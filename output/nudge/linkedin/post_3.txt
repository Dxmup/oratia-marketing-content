"OUR FEE IS 20%" AND "YOU KEEP 80%" ARE THE SAME SENTENCE

Owners don't experience them the same way.

Thaler and Sunstein draw on decades of behavioral research to show that logically identical information produces systematically different decisions when framed differently. The frame is not neutral packaging. It is part of the offer.

In VRM management, the fee conversation has two common frames:

Frame A: "Our management fee is 20% of gross booking revenue."

Frame B: "You'll keep roughly 80% of your gross booking revenue. That structure covers guest communication, maintenance coordination, cleaning oversight, and your nightly rate optimization."

The math is identical. The owner's psychological experience of the number is not.

Frame A is a cost. The owner is evaluating how much they are giving up. Loss aversion, which Thaler's research shows is roughly twice as powerful as the equivalent gain, makes the number feel large.

Frame B is an outcome. The owner is evaluating what they receive and what they retain. Gain orientation, even modest gain orientation, evaluates more favorably.

THE REFRAME IN PRACTICE

This does not mean hiding the fee. It means sequencing and framing it around the owner's result.

"Your net income on this property last year was roughly X. Here is what we project it at under management, after our fee. That's the number I'd ask you to focus on."

Or, for a self-managing owner: "You're managing this yourself and investing time in it every week. Here's what that time is worth in your billing rate, and here's what our fee actually costs relative to the time you'd recover."

Field note: The framing effect is most pronounced with Investor owners, who enter the conversation in evaluation mode. They are already building a mental P&L, and they will anchor to whichever frame you give them. Framing around the return, not the cost, aligns with how they are already thinking.

The fee conversation is not about the number. It's about the frame.
