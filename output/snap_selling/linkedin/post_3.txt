BRING AN INSIGHT, NOT A PROJECTION

Two operators pitch the same owner. One gets the listing.

The one who doesn't leads with a revenue projection showing 12% better average nightly rate against the owner's current performance. It's accurate. It's formatted well. The owner nods politely and says they'll think about it.

The one who gets it walks in and says:

"I pulled your listing before I came. Your overall rating is a 4.6, which is solid. But your cleanliness score is a 4.2. Every comparable in your neighborhood running a 4.8 or above on cleanliness is commanding 18% more per night on average. You've got roughly a $9,000 annual gap that a cleaning protocol change can close. That's before any pricing or distribution work."

Same market. Same owner. Different result.

THE DATA VERSUS INSIGHT DISTINCTION

Jill Konrath's SNAP Selling draws a clear line between information and insight. Information is data the buyer could find themselves. Insight is something the buyer doesn't know that changes how they see their own situation.

A revenue projection is information. The owner could run it on AirDNA. They've probably already seen something like it.

A cleanliness gap tied to a specific dollar figure from their specific property's review history is an insight. They couldn't find that without you.

One makes you another vendor with a spreadsheet. The other makes you someone who understands their asset in a way they don't.

WHAT APPOINTMENT PREP ACTUALLY LOOKS LIKE

Before every first appointment with a new owner:

Read their reviews, all of them. Look for patterns in what guests flag. Identify the specific thing that's costing them the most in score or revenue that a management change could fix. Pull comparable properties that are performing better and find the one variable that's different. Walk in with a specific number, not a general range.

This is not a lot of work. Thirty minutes of targeted preparation separates the operators who earn trust from the operators who ask for it.

Field note: Investor owners respond to this approach most directly because their decision is fundamentally financial. Steward owners respond to a different version of the same insight: not the revenue gap, but the guest experience gap. "Your reviews show guests consistently mentioning they couldn't reach anyone for maintenance questions. That's costing you repeat bookings from the guests most likely to treat the property the way you'd want." Same technique, different lever.

Script: "Before I walk you through how we work, I want to share one thing I found when I looked at your listing. I think it's the most direct thing we could fix."

Then show them the insight. Let them ask how you fix it. That's when the real appointment starts.
