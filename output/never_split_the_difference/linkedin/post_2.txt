YOUR PRODUCT DOESN'T MATTER UNTIL THE PROBLEM DOES

Many VRM operators start listing appointments entirely backward.

Sitting at the kitchen island of a prospective owner, the instinct is to immediately showcase the toolkit: dynamic pricing, 24/7 maintenance dispatch, professional photography, guest vetting technology. You're presenting the solution before the owner has acknowledged the problem. This is especially costly for operators who are still building their brand or can't yet compete on scale.

OWNERS DON'T BUY SERVICES

Owners don't buy software. They don't buy cleaning protocols. They buy the alleviation of specific felt pain. If they don't actively feel the pain of midnight guest calls, the anxiety of a bad review cycle, or the frustration of stagnant revenue, your solution has no context. The feature list sounds like a list of expenses, not answers.

This is what Kazanjy calls problem-centric selling, and it's especially critical when the product or brand isn't the most powerful thing in the room. A sharp problem diagnosis beats a polished pitch deck every time.

THE EXCAVATION QUESTIONS

Before you describe a single thing your company does, excavate the current state. Let the owner feel the weight of their own situation.

- "Tell me about the worst guest experience you've had with this property." Don't rush the story. Let the details surface.
- "How much time do you spend on guest messaging in an average week?" Many owners underestimate this by a factor of two.
- "What was your actual net income last year, after cleaning fees, supplies, emergency maintenance, and your own labor?" Very few owners have run this number honestly.
- "What did you have to cancel or miss personally because the property needed your attention last year?"

Script: "Before I tell you anything about what we do, I want to make sure I actually understand your situation. Tell me about the last time this property created a real problem for you, something that cost you time, money, or peace of mind. I'd rather understand what's happening before I talk about what we offer."

Once they've described their operational drag, in their own words and with their own emotional investment, the solution doesn't need to be sold. It needs to be presented as the obvious escape from what they just described.

Field note: This approach levels the playing field for operators who can't compete on brand scale or portfolio size. A smaller operator who diagnoses the pain more precisely than a larger competitor wins on authority, not resources. The owner isn't buying a company. They're buying relief from a specific problem. Know the problem better than anyone in the room.
