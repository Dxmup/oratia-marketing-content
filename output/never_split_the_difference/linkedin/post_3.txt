DOOR COUNT IS A VANITY METRIC

The most expensive mistake in vacation rental management is accepting an owner you should have declined.

The assumption that growth means saying yes to every property that crosses your desk is a growth trap. Not all revenue is created equal. A demanding, micro-managing owner with an underperforming property will consume 80% of your operational bandwidth while contributing 20% or less of your margin.

THE REAL COST OF A BAD FIT

The financial cost is real. The systemic cost is worse:

Your team's morale erodes when they know this owner will complain regardless of the outcome.
Response times for your good owners slip because the bad-fit owner occupies disproportionate attention.
Your property manager's confidence degrades over months of thankless effort.
Your best team members eventually leave for an operator who doesn't accept these properties.
And if you're the one doing the managing, you become the operational constraint, unable to grow because you're stuck firefighting a client you should never have taken.

QUALIFICATION BEFORE THE APPOINTMENT

Build your disqualifying questions into the discovery call, before you drive to the property. Filter upstream.

- Does the property meet your minimum annual revenue threshold at a realistic occupancy rate?
- Is the owner prepared to be fully hands-off on operational decisions?
- Do their expectations for both revenue and care align with what the market actually supports?
- Are they willing to accept your guest vetting standards, or do they want to approve individual bookings?
- What is their personal use window during peak season, and does it conflict with your revenue strategy for the property?

If the answers aren't aligned, decline before you invest another hour.

Script: "I want to be straightforward with you. Based on what you've described, I don't think we're the best fit for this property. Our model is built for owners who want a full handoff, and from what you've told me, you'd be most comfortable staying involved in a few key decisions. That's completely reasonable. It's just not the model we're built around. I'd rather tell you that today than sign a contract we'd both regret in six months. I can point you toward two operators whose model actually matches what you're looking for."

The operator who says no to a bad fit gains three things: time and bandwidth for good owners, a market reputation for selectivity that raises perceived value, and occasionally a referral from the owner they turned down, who will respect the honesty and send someone who is actually a fit.

Profitable door count is the metric. Door count is a distraction.
