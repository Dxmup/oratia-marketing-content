OWNERS DON'T BUY YOUR SOFTWARE. THEY BUY WHAT YOUR SOFTWARE PRODUCES. MOST VRM PITCHES GET THIS BACKWARDS.

Decision-makers, the people who actually control a buying decision, think in outcomes. They do not think in features.

They want to know: What does my life look like after I switch? What changes? What gets better? What gets easier?

They do not want to know what software you use, how many properties you manage, or how your team is structured, at least not in the first five minutes. That's not how their brain is processing the decision.

Anthony Parinello spent his career studying how senior executives make decisions and what language actually reaches them. His core finding: feature language requires the listener to do translation work. Outcome language does the work for them.

In VRM acquisition, this gap is enormous.

THE TRANSLATION EXERCISE

Here is how this shows up in practice. Left side is how most operators say it. Right side is the outcome version:

"We use dynamic pricing software." vs. "Owners who switch to us typically see a 15 to 22 percent revenue increase in their first season."

"We have a 24/7 guest communication line." vs. "Most owners tell us their after-hours calls drop by 90 percent within 60 days."

"We have an owner portal so you can track everything." vs. "You get real-time visibility without emailing your manager every time you want an update."

"Our onboarding is thorough and efficient." vs. "Most owners are fully transitioned in under three weeks with no gap in guest bookings."

Same service. Different register. Different impact on the person deciding.

HOW TO USE THIS

Before your next listing appointment, take every sentence in your standard pitch that starts with "We..." and rewrite it as an outcome the owner experiences.

Not what you do. What changes for them when you do it.

The owner who is evaluating you is not doing a feature comparison. They are asking one question: Is switching to this manager worth the disruption?

Outcome language answers that question. Feature language forces them to answer it themselves.
