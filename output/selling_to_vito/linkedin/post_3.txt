WANTING THE LISTING TOO VISIBLY IS A CLOSING LIABILITY

There is a version of enthusiasm in listing appointments that costs you the deal.

It's not obvious enthusiasm. It's the subtle kind: thanking the owner for their time, softening your fee when they push back, qualifying your claims when they seem skeptical, and presenting yourself as lucky to have the opportunity.

Anthony Parinello calls the underlying concept Equal Business Stature. His argument is that the best sales conversations happen between people who see themselves as equals. The moment you sell from below, the other person stops treating you like someone who can solve their problem.

In VRM, this plays out in a specific way.

THE LISTING APPOINTMENT THAT SIGNALS DESPERATION

When an operator needs the listing, the owner can sense it. Not always consciously. But the signals are there: a fee that adjusts too quickly, a point of view that softens when challenged, a closing line that sounds like a plea.

Owners are handing over a major asset. The question they are unconsciously asking throughout the appointment is: Is this manager someone I trust to handle my property when things go wrong?

An operator who adjusts their position under pressure signals that they will adjust their standards under pressure too.

THE POSTURE SHIFT

Before the appointment starts, decide what your walk-away condition is. What kind of owner or property is not a good fit for you? What would make you say no to this listing?

Having a real answer to those questions changes your posture in the room.

Then open with something like this:

Script: "Before I show you what we do, I want to make sure we're the right fit for each other. We're selective about the properties we take on, and I have a few questions that help me figure that out. Can I start there?"

The owner's posture shifts in the first sixty seconds. You are no longer presenting. You are evaluating. The dynamic is different.

Field note: This opener works especially well with the Investor owner type, who respects directness and often tests for it. A Delegator may respond better to slightly warmer framing: the spirit of the question is the same, but the tone is collaborative rather than evaluating.
