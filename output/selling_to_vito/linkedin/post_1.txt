THE BEST LISTING APPOINTMENT I EVER LOST ENDED WITH THE OWNER SAYING HE WAS INTERESTED

He was. He walked me out. He shook my hand. He said he'd be in touch.

His wife called three weeks later to say they'd decided to stay with their current manager.

His wife. Who I had never spoken to. Who had opinions about the switch that I had never heard. Who was, in retrospect, the actual decision-maker.

I had run a great pitch for the wrong person.

Anthony Parinello calls this the VITO problem. VITO stands for Very Important Top Officer, and his core argument is that most salespeople never actually reach the person who controls the decision. They reach the person who answers the call, agrees to the meeting, and says things like "this sounds great, let me think about it."

The person who can't say yes without checking with someone else is not the decision-maker.

THE VRM VERSION

This shows up constantly in property management. A married couple co-owns a property. One partner is the point of contact. The other, often the spouse, handles the financial decisions, the home decisions, or both.

You pitch one of them. They say it all sounds good. Then you don't hear back.

Or: An investor owns several properties and has a real estate attorney managing trust issues. The investor likes your pitch. The attorney has objections that never surface in your conversation. You lose the deal without knowing why.

The operator who identified the real decision-maker early, and found a way to include them in the conversation, ran a fundamentally different appointment.

THE FIRST FIVE MINUTES

One question changes everything: "Is there anyone else who should be part of this conversation before we get into the details?"

It sounds simple. It is. But too few operators ask it.

If the answer is yes, stop. Reschedule with all parties present, or send a summary specifically designed for the person who wasn't in the room.

If the answer is no, now you know you have the decision-maker in front of you and you can pitch accordingly.

Field note: This question lands differently with a Skeptic owner. For a Skeptic, reframe it: "Sometimes these decisions involve more than one person and I want to make sure I'm talking to the right person before we go further." The framing implies respect for their time, not doubt about their authority.
