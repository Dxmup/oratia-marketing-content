THE CORE PROBLEM SELLING TO VITO IDENTIFIES

Parinello's observation is counterintuitive until you've experienced it: most salespeople never actually reach the person who owns the decision. They reach someone adjacent. A spouse, a partner, a property trustee, an adult child managing an elderly parent's investment. That person schedules the meeting, listens attentively, says all the right things, and then delivers a second-hand version of your pitch to the person who actually decides.

In VRM acquisition, this is epidemic. And it is almost never on purpose. The operator isn't deliberately trying to miss the decision-maker. They simply haven't developed the habit of asking who else should be in the room.

The fix is three behaviors applied consistently:

BEHAVIOR 1: IDENTIFY THE DECISION STRUCTURE BEFORE THE APPOINTMENT

The best time to find out who is involved in the decision is before you show up. In the scheduling call or email, ask: "Before we meet, can I ask who else is typically part of a decision like this for you?"

Most owners will answer straightforwardly. Some will say they handle it themselves. Others will mention a spouse, a financial advisor, or a business partner. Now you know before you start.

If multiple people are involved, ask whether they can both be present. Offer a flexible scheduling option to make it easy. If that's not possible, build your appointment summary specifically for the person who wasn't there.

BEHAVIOR 2: TRANSLATE EVERY SERVICE DESCRIPTION INTO AN OUTCOME STATEMENT

The feature-to-outcome translation exercise from Post 2 is worth doing in writing before every significant appointment.

Take each thing you plan to say about how you work. Then ask: what does the owner experience or not have to experience because of this?

"We have a dedicated maintenance coordinator" becomes "Owners who switch to us typically stop getting maintenance calls. We handle coordination end to end, and owners hear from us when it's resolved, not when it's ongoing."

"We do professional photography for every listing" becomes "Most of our listings rent at a higher nightly rate than their neighborhood average because first impressions in search results are the primary driver of click-through."

"We have a 4.9 guest review score" becomes "Most guests who stay with our properties book through us again. Which means your property stays booked without relying entirely on first-time guests."

The translation takes fifteen minutes per appointment. It is the highest-leverage fifteen minutes in your prep.

BEHAVIOR 3: WALK INTO EVERY APPOINTMENT WITH A QUALIFYING QUESTION READY

Parinello's Equal Business Stature principle is a posture, not a technique. But the posture has to be grounded in something real. The qualifying question is how you make EBS concrete.

What would make this listing not a good fit for you?

Have a real answer. Then ask the owner a version of it in the first five minutes. Something like: "We're selective about the properties we take on, mostly because the model works best when the owner wants a full handoff. Can I ask a few questions to see if that's what you're looking for?"

Now you are evaluating. The dynamic is different from the start.

THE VITO PERSONALITY MATRIX APPLIED TO VRM OWNER TYPES

Parinello identifies a specific set of traits for the VITO: direct, bottom-line focused, results-driven, time-scarce, and uncomfortable with subordinate sellers.

Not every VRM owner maps to the full VITO profile. Here is how the six owner types from the VRM Sales Library framework map onto Parinello's model:

INVESTOR: Full VITO equivalent. Makes decisions fast. Wants the revenue number and the risk assessment in the first five minutes. Outcome language is essential from the opening sentence. Equal Business Stature is not optional with this owner: they will test for it, and they will disrespect an operator who softens under pressure.

DELEGATOR: VITO with low urgency. They want to hand everything off, which makes them a good fit in theory, but they don't feel the friction of the status quo the way the Investor does. Outcome language still matters, but the outcome they care most about is peace of mind, not revenue. Adjust accordingly.

STEWARD: Not a VITO. This is a relationship decision, not a financial one. Feature-to-outcome translation still applies, but the outcome that moves a Steward is trust, not efficiency. "Your property will be managed the way you would manage it" is more persuasive than any revenue projection.

SKEPTIC: Gatekeeper-adjacent. The Skeptic may be the decision-maker, but they often defer to data or to a third party (an accountant, an advisor, a trusted neighbor) before committing. The hidden decision-maker question is critical with this owner type. Find out who they will check with and build your case for that conversation, not just the one you're in.

CONTROLLER: VITO with blocking instincts. This owner has authority and uses it, but they use it to veto rather than to approve. Outcome language matters less than process transparency. The Controller needs to understand exactly how decisions get made in their property's management, and they need reassurance that they can intervene if something goes wrong.

OPPORTUNIST: VITO with a short time horizon. The Opportunist is making a seasonal or transitional decision. They are not thinking about a multi-year relationship. Outcome language focused on the immediate season, not a long-term partnership, is more persuasive than anything that implies ongoing commitment.
