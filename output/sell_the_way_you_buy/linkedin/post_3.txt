THE FEE CONVERSATION HAPPENS BEFORE YOU PRESENT THE FEE

The management fee objection is not a price problem. It is almost always a framing problem.

The owner who says "your fee seems high" is comparing your number to something. If you have not established what they are comparing it to, they compare it to the cheapest option they have seen, or to an abstract sense of what "should" cost something. Both comparisons favor your competition.

Priemer draws on behavioral economics to show that framing determines how any number lands. The same fee presented against different reference points produces different emotional responses.

ESTABLISHING THE FRAME BEFORE THE NUMBER

Before presenting your management fee, you need to establish three things:

1. What the owner is currently spending on self-management or their current manager. Time is money. An owner self-managing is spending 8 to 15 hours per week on guest communication, maintenance coordination, calendar management, and incident response. At any reasonable rate for their time, that number is significant.

Script: "Before I walk you through our fee structure, I want to understand what you're currently putting into the property each week. How much time would you say you're spending on guest communication, maintenance calls, and general management?"

2. What the owner is currently earning versus what's available in the market. If comparable properties are producing 20% more per year, the difference is your fee many times over.

Script: "Based on what you described about your current revenue, I want to show you where this property sits relative to comps in your area. There's a gap here worth talking about before we discuss what it costs to close it."

3. What incidents or stress they are absorbing that they should not be absorbing. The 11 PM call from a guest who locked themselves out. The maintenance issue that sat unresolved for two weeks. These are costs. They just don't appear on a spreadsheet.

WHEN TO PRESENT THE FEE

Present the fee only after the owner has articulated at least one of the above. The fee is now a trade, not a cost. The question is not "does this seem like a lot" but "does this trade make sense."

Field note: Investor owners respond most immediately to the revenue gap framing. Steward owners respond to the stress cost framing. Both need to believe the fee is a reallocation of existing cost before they can evaluate it clearly.
