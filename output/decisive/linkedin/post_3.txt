OWNERS SAY THEY WANT HANDS-OFF MANAGEMENT. THEIR BEHAVIOR SAYS SOMETHING DIFFERENT.

The listing appointment question "are you comfortable with a fully managed relationship?" has a near-universal answer: yes, of course. The owner who calls every week in month 2 also said yes.

Chip and Dan Heath call the fix "ooching": running a small experiment or asking a behavior-revealing question before committing to a big decision. In VRM, that means replacing stated-preference questions with reality-test questions.

THE SWAP

Stated preference: "Are you comfortable with us making day-to-day decisions without checking in?"
Reality test: "Tell me about the last time something went wrong with the property and how you handled it."

The story the owner tells reveals their actual communication patterns, anxiety threshold, and the relationship they'll want when things get difficult. The "yes, I'm hands-off" answer reveals nothing.

Stated preference: "Are you okay with our standard maintenance response window?"
Reality test: "What would you want to know if a maintenance issue came in on a Friday afternoon?"

The second question tells you exactly what the owner's expectations are, and whether your standard process matches them.

More reality-test questions that surface actual behavior:

"What does your current communication pattern look like with whoever manages or monitors this property now?"

"Tell me about a guest experience that frustrated you. What was your reaction?"

"What is the most important thing a property manager could get wrong with this property?"

The answers are not always disqualifying. Sometimes they are. More often, they reveal the specific expectations to address in the onboarding conversation so the relationship starts on accurate footing rather than optimistic assumptions.

Field note: Controller owners will often tell you they want to stay involved, and they are at least being honest about it. The operator who hears "I want to stay pretty involved" and builds the operating structure around that expectation delivers a better relationship than the operator who tries to shift the Controller toward a hands-off model.
