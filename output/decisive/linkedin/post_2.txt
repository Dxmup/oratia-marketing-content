AFTER A GOOD LISTING APPOINTMENT, YOU ARE NOT OBJECTIVE

Chip and Dan Heath's research on confirmation bias shows that once a direction is set, people process new information selectively. Evidence supporting the preferred conclusion gets amplified. Evidence contradicting it gets explained away or forgotten.

After a warm listing appointment with a multi-property owner, the BD who walks back to the office isn't running an objective evaluation. They're building a case. Everything positive about the meeting is remembered clearly. The owner's pointed question about maintenance response times gets filed under "understandably thorough." The spouse who said nothing for 45 minutes gets filed under "probably just the quieter partner."

This isn't carelessness. It's how post-decision cognition works.

THE DISCONFIRMATION REVIEW

The intervention is simple: before building the case to proceed, run a disconfirmation check. The goal is not to talk yourself out of a good opportunity. It's to surface the signals that enthusiasm suppressed and evaluate them with a cooler mind.

Five questions worth asking after every listing appointment:

What did the owner say or do that should give us pause?

How did they respond when I described our maintenance response window?

What was their reaction when I mentioned pricing adjustments in shoulder or off-season?

Did anything in how they described their last manager suggest a pattern that concerns me?

What did the quietest person in the room communicate through their body language or their few questions?

These questions don't surface problems in every appointment. When they do, it is almost always something the BD noticed in the moment and filed under "not a big deal."

Field note: This review works best with a second person, a manager or partner who wasn't in the appointment. The BD is too close to it. The external question, "what else did they say about the maintenance situation?", asked by someone who wasn't there, often surfaces details the BD had rationalized rather than evaluated.
