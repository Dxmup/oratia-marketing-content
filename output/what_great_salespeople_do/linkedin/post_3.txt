THE OWNER SAID THE FEE WAS TOO HIGH. THEY DIDN'T MEAN IT.

When an owner raises a fee objection in a listing appointment, the standard response is to explain what the fee covers. Break down the services. Compare to market rate. Maybe offer a different tier.

None of that works. Not because the explanation is wrong, but because fee was never the real objection.

Bosworth and Zoldan's research on empathy in sales shows that buyers rarely state the actual source of their resistance. They state a surface objection that's socially acceptable to raise. The real objection is usually emotional: fear, doubt, identity, prior experience. The fee is just the container they put it in.

THE QUESTION UNDERNEATH THE OBJECTION

When an owner says "your fee is higher than the other manager I spoke with," the statement worth investigating is usually one of these:

"I'm not sure I can trust you enough to justify the premium."
"I'm afraid I'm making a mistake and I need more reassurance."
"I've been burned before and I'm looking for a reason to say no."
"I'm not convinced your service is actually different."

Answering the fee question doesn't address any of those. You just gave them more information about a belief they haven't changed yet.

WHAT TO SAY INSTEAD

Script: "It sounds like you want to make sure this is the right move before you commit. What would make you feel more confident about it?"

That question does the real work. It names the emotional state the owner is actually in. It gives them permission to say what they're actually worried about. And it moves the conversation from your pitch to their decision-making process.

Field note: Investor owners who raise fee objections are often running a trust calculation, not a cost analysis. They have the margin to pay the fee. They don't yet have enough confidence in you to justify it. More data doesn't resolve a trust problem. A direct question about what would build their confidence does.

The operators who consistently win competitive listings have learned that objection handling is not about having better answers. It is about asking better questions.
