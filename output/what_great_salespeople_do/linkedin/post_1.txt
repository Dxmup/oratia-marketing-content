THE LISTING APPOINTMENT FAILS BEFORE THE PITCH STARTS

Most VRM operators lose listing appointments not because their pitch was weak, but because they started pitching before the owner was ready to hear it.

Bosworth and Zoldan map out four emotional states buyers move through before making a decision: unawareness, awareness of discomfort, recognition of the gap, and openness to change. The operator who shows up with a revenue projection and a feature list is pitching to stage four. Many of the owners sitting across from you are still at stage two.

This is the core mistake in VRM owner acquisition. Not the pitch itself. The sequence.

THE FOUR STAGES IN YOUR LISTING APPOINTMENT

Stage 1: Unawareness. The owner senses something is off but hasn't named it yet. Your job is not to pitch. It's to ask questions that surface the discomfort.

Stage 2: Awareness of discomfort. The owner knows something isn't working. This sounds like readiness. It isn't. They haven't decided they want to change.

Stage 3: Recognition of the gap. The owner can articulate what they want that they don't have. This is the first stage where data starts to land.

Stage 4: Openness to change. The owner has resolved the emotional calculus and is genuinely evaluating options. This is where your pitch belongs.

WHAT THIS LOOKS LIKE IN THE ROOM

Most operators walk in and immediately pitch stage four. The owner hasn't gotten there yet. The owner hears the pitch, nods along, says "let me think about it," and ghosts.

The operator thinks they lost on price or competition. They lost on sequence.

The operator who opens with "tell me what's been going on with the property" and then follows the owner's answer with "what specifically is getting to be a lot?" is doing the diagnostic work that earns the right to pitch.

Field note: Skeptic owners are almost always in stage two at the start of a listing appointment. They've been burned before. Their discomfort is real but their openness to change is low. Skipping the diagnostic and pitching a Skeptic at stage four is the single most reliable way to lose that listing.

The pitch isn't the problem. The sequence is.
