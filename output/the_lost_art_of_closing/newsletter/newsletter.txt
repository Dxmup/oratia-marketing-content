This week covered Anthony Iannarino's The Lost Art of Closing across three posts. The common thread: closing is a sequence of commitments built throughout the acquisition process, not a skill applied at the end of a presentation.

Post 1 introduced the framework. Post 2 drilled into the commitment to explore change and why agreeing to a meeting is not the same thing. Post 3 addressed the consensus problem: the deals that die in conversations the operator was never part of.

This newsletter goes deeper on the full commitment sequence and gives you a diagnostic for your own process.

---

THE FULL COMMITMENT STACK

Iannarino identifies a series of commitments that must be secured before you earn the right to ask for the final one. Not every deal requires all of them in sequence, but most stalled deals are missing at least one.

Here is the full stack with VRM applications:

COMMITMENT 1: EXPLORE CHANGE

The owner must commit to the idea that their current situation is worth reconsidering. This is not the same as agreeing to a meeting. An owner who shows up out of curiosity or social obligation has not committed to change.

Diagnostic question for your last deal: Did the owner explicitly say they were open to changing their current situation, or did you infer it from their agreement to meet?

If you inferred it, the first commitment was never secured.

COMMITMENT 2: COLLABORATE WITH REAL INFORMATION

The owner must be willing to share their actual situation: current occupancy rate, prior management experience, real concerns about switching, revenue expectations. An owner who will not share this information with you during the evaluation process will not share it after signing. They will be difficult to manage and difficult to retain.

Use collaboration as a filter. Ask directly for the information you need to give them useful advice. Watch what they do.

Script: "To give you an accurate picture of what professional management would do for this property, I need to understand where it is right now. Are you comfortable sharing your last 12 months of occupancy and revenue?"

The owner who says no has told you something important.

COMMITMENT 3: BUILD CONSENSUS

The owner must commit to bringing all relevant decision-makers into the process before you advance. Post 3 this week covered this in detail.

The one question to ask at every first meeting: "Is there anyone else involved in this decision who should be part of this conversation?"

Ask it before you leave. Do not wait for the stall.

COMMITMENT 4: RESOLVE CONCERNS

Objections at the close are almost always concerns that went underground earlier in the process. The operator who creates explicit space for concerns to surface throughout the conversation is not inviting problems. They are preventing the worse version: the concern that kills the deal after the proposal is in.

An owner who says "I'm a little worried about guest vetting" during the appointment is giving you a gift. Address it fully. Then confirm it is resolved.

Script: "Does that fully address your concern about how we vet guests, or is there something else there we should work through before we go further?"

The check-in question is what most operators skip. The owner says "sure" and the operator moves on without confirming resolution. That "sure" is not the same as "yes, I'm satisfied." Confirm it explicitly.

COMMITMENT 5: INVEST

The owner must consciously commit to the investment before they sign. This means walking through every element of the financial relationship clearly: management fee, onboarding costs, maintenance reserve, and any other recurring costs. Do not rush past this.

Owners who sign without fully processing the investment become regret-prone clients. They question every maintenance bill. They push back on fees they agreed to in writing. They were not ready to invest and you let them sign anyway.

Give the investment conversation its full time. Then ask: "Is the financial structure clear and workable for you, or do you have questions about any part of it?"

COMMITMENT 6: DECIDE

Asking for a decision is not pressure if the prior commitments have been secured. It is the logical conclusion of a complete process.

End the appointment with a clear timeline: "We have a limited number of properties we take on in this market each quarter. I'd like to hold your spot while you finalize the decision. Does two weeks give you enough time?"

Two things happen with that ask. The owner who is genuinely interested will say yes and you have a real deadline. The owner who was not serious will reveal it, which is also useful information.

---

THE DIAGNOSTIC

Run this against your last three deals that stalled or went quiet after a good appointment:

1. Did the owner explicitly commit to exploring change, or did you infer it from their willingness to meet?
2. Did the owner share their current financials and operating history during the evaluation?
3. Did you ask who else is involved in the decision before you left the first meeting?
4. Did you create explicit space for concerns and confirm each one was resolved before advancing?
5. Did you walk through the full investment clearly and confirm they were ready for it?
6. Did you ask for a decision with a specific timeline?

Most operators who run this diagnostic on a lost deal find two or three missing commitments. The missed close was not bad luck. It was a structural gap.
