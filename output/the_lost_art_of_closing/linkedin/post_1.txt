THE CLOSE IS NOT A MOMENT. IT'S A SEQUENCE.

Many VRM operators lose listing appointments they thought they had won.

The appointment went well. The owner seemed engaged. The revenue projection landed. Then they said "let me think about it," and nothing came back.

The usual diagnosis is that the pitch failed. The actual problem is usually earlier.

Anthony Iannarino's The Lost Art of Closing makes a point that most salespeople resist: closing is not a single ask at the end of a conversation. It is a sequence of smaller commitments built throughout the entire relationship. By the time you ask for the contract, the owner should have already said yes to a series of smaller things.

WHAT THE COMMITMENT SEQUENCE LOOKS LIKE

Before your pitch, the owner should have committed to the idea that change is worth exploring. Not just that they agreed to a meeting. Those are different things.

During your conversation, they should have committed to sharing real information: financials, prior management history, actual concerns. An owner who gives you nothing to work with during the appointment will give you nothing after the proposal.

By the end, they should have committed to including all decision-makers, surfacing concerns so they can be resolved, and deciding in a defined timeframe.

If any of those commitments are missing, the deal will stall. The stall is not bad luck. It is the missing commitment surfacing.

WHERE OPERATORS BREAK DOWN

The most common gap is the first one. Operators treat agreement to meet as agreement to evaluate change. It is not.

A self-managing owner who agreed to "just hear you out" has not committed to professional management. A dissatisfied owner has not necessarily committed to switching. Operators who skip the step of getting the owner to name the problem and commit to solving it are pitching on a foundation that was never set.

The presentation quality does not fix this. What fixes it is asking early: "Is professional management something you are genuinely open to right now, or are you still figuring out whether it makes sense?"

That question is uncomfortable. It is also the one that tells you whether you are in a deal.

Field note: Iannarino's framework works because it reframes closing as a process of securing agreement throughout, not a skill applied at the end. Operators who treat it as a process close more deals and spend less time on proposals that were never going to convert.
