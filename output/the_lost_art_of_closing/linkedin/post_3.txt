THE DEAL THAT DIES IN A CONVERSATION YOU WERE NOT IN

Many VRM listing appointments go well. The operator presents clearly, the owner engages, the numbers work, and everyone leaves feeling positive.

Then nothing happens.

The owner says they need to talk to their spouse. Or their business partner. Or the sibling who co-owns the property. And the operator waits for a follow-up call that does not come, from a conversation they had no part in.

Anthony Iannarino calls this the consensus problem. You built agreement with one decision-maker. But there were others.

THE CO-OWNERSHIP REALITY IN VRM

Vacation properties are frequently co-owned. A husband and wife who bought it together. Two business partners who use it as a shared investment. Siblings who inherited it. The person who took your call and scheduled the appointment is rarely the only person whose opinion will determine whether you get the contract.

The operator who presents only to the person in the room is leaving the outcome to a conversation they cannot influence.

One question changes this. Ask it before you leave the first meeting:

Script: "Before we go, I want to make sure I'm being helpful to everyone involved in this decision. Is there a partner, spouse, or co-owner who will be part of this? I want to make sure they have the same information you do, and if it would be useful, I'm happy to set up a time to walk through it together."

That question is not pressure. It is respect for the other decision-makers and self-protection for the operator.

WHY THIS MATTERS BEYOND THE CLOSE

The owner who says "let me talk to my wife" and then goes quiet is often not avoiding you. They are navigating a real internal conversation that you gave them no tools for. Their spouse has concerns that were never addressed. Their business partner has a competing preference. Their sibling thinks they should self-manage.

None of those concerns are impossible to address. But you cannot address them in a conversation you were never invited to.

The operator who brings all decision-makers into the process early closes more deals and has fewer mystery losses. They also avoid onboarding clients who had a co-owner that never fully agreed.

Field note: The best version of this ask is neutral and logistical, not a sign you are chasing. Framing it as wanting to be helpful to everyone involved changes the register. Most owners respond well to it.
