THE FIRST NUMBER IN THE ROOM WINS THE FEE NEGOTIATION

Owners don't evaluate management fees on merit. They anchor to the first number they hear and measure everything else against it.

Dan Ariely's research on arbitrary coherence shows that the initial price in any negotiation becomes a reference point that shapes all subsequent judgments, even when that anchor is arbitrary. The owner is not running a spreadsheet in their head. They are asking: is this number higher or lower than the one I already have?

That has a direct consequence for VRM listing appointments.

If an owner has already spoken with a competitor before meeting you, your fee doesn't land in a vacuum. It lands against the anchor the competitor set. If they quoted 10%, your 12% isn't being evaluated against your value stack. It's being evaluated against 10%. You are already defending upward before you've said a word about what you actually deliver.

WHAT THIS MEANS FOR THE APPOINTMENT

The operator who names a number first with a clear value frame owns the anchor. The operator who goes second is always in comparison mode.

This isn't a pitch trick. It's a structural advantage that has nothing to do with how good your management actually is.

The practical moves:

Name your fee early, before the owner has a chance to introduce what they've seen elsewhere. Frame it alongside what it includes, so the anchor is not just a number but a value package.

When you learn the owner has spoken with other managers, ask what they were quoted before you name your fee. That tells you what anchor you're working against and gives you the option to reset it deliberately rather than accidentally walking into a comparison.

If the conversation has already anchored to a lower number, don't attack the number. Attack the comparison: "The question isn't whether 10% is cheaper. The question is whether what's included in that 10% covers what actually happens to a property in this market."

Field note: This dynamic is most acute with Investor owners, who approach the conversation analytically and will build a mental spreadsheet the moment a fee is mentioned. With Steward owners, the fee is secondary to trust signals, and anchoring matters less than demonstrating that you'll care for the property the way they do.
