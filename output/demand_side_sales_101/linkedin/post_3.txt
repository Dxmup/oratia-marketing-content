YOUR DECK IS NOT THE PROBLEM. LEADING WITH IT IS.

Most VRM listing appointments are a supply-side pitch in a demand-side conversation.

Supply-side: here's what we offer, here's what we charge, here's what your property will earn. The operator leads with a deck: services, fees, revenue projections.

Demand-side: here's what I'm trying to accomplish, here's the context of this property, here's what I need from whoever I hire. The owner arrives with this framing. Usually unsaid.

The operator who pitches supply-side into a demand-side conversation wonders why the best deck doesn't always win.

THE FLIP

Bob Moesta's framework: what job is the owner trying to hire for? Not what features do they prefer, but what specific job have they brought you in to do?

Two owners. Same three-bedroom property, same market. The owner who bought eight months ago as a yield asset needs you to hit a number. The owner who inherited the property from a parent needs someone who will treat it like it matters. A revenue projection in that second conversation is tone-deaf.

Same deck. Completely different jobs.

THE MOVE

Put the deck down before you open it. Ask three questions:

"Tell me about the property. When did you get it and what were you originally trying to do with it?"

"What's changed since then?"

"What does success look like six months from now, not just for the property, but for you personally?"

The answers tell you what job you're being hired to do. Then your pitch maps directly to that job.

WHY THIS WORKS

The operator who asks these questions before pitching looks more competent than the operator with the polished presentation. Not because questions signal competence, but because they signal something the deck never can: that you're trying to understand their situation before you try to solve it.

Many owners are skeptical that any manager actually does this. Genuinely doing it, not as a warmup before the slides come out, differentiates you before you've said a word about your services.

Script: "Before I tell you anything about us, I want to understand the property and what you're actually trying to accomplish. The managers who do well for owners are the ones who match what the owner needs. So let's start there."

Then actually start there.

Field note: The Investor owner will sometimes push back. They came with specific questions and want to compare numbers. Have the data ready. But even with an Investor, open with one context question before the deck: "What's the story on this property?" They'll answer. The answer usually sharpens the pitch.
