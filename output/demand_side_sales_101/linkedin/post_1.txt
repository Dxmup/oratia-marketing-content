THE LISTING APPOINTMENT WENT WELL. THEN NOTHING.

The owner was engaged. They asked good questions. They seemed ready. Then a week went by. You sent a follow-up with an updated revenue projection. No response.

Many operators treat a stalled deal as a persuasion problem. So they pitch harder: better numbers, a limited-time fee offer. The deal still doesn't move.

Bob Moesta's demand-side framework explains why. The stall isn't a pitch failure. It's a forces diagnostic failure.

THE FOUR FORCES

Every switching decision is governed by four forces simultaneously:

Push: frustration with the current situation driving the owner away from where they are.

Pull: the attraction of what you're offering.

Anxiety: fear of making a wrong move. That the transition goes badly, that guest bookings get disrupted, that you're worse than what they already have.

Habit: the inertia of staying put. Even a dissatisfied owner has to notify guests, negotiate the transition, and explain the change to a co-owner.

A deal stalls when anxiety and habit outweigh push and pull. Pitching harder adds more pull. But if the block is anxiety or habit, more pull doesn't move the deal.

THE DIAGNOSTIC

Ask one question: "What's holding you back from moving forward?"

Habit sounds like: "We've been with them a long time." "It's just not the right time."

Anxiety sounds like: "I'm worried about the guest bookings during the transition." "I've heard mixed things about switching managers."

THE RESPONSE

Habit blocks respond to friction reduction.

Script: "One of the biggest reasons owners stay with a manager they're unhappy with is just the effort of switching. We've done this enough times to have a clean transition process. Here's what we handle so you don't have to."

Anxiety blocks respond to risk reversal.

Script: "Most owners worry about signing with someone new and having it go sideways. So: we protect all existing bookings at our commission rate, we guarantee a 24-hour response to any owner concern, and if you're not satisfied with our communication in the first 90 days, you can terminate with no penalty."

Most of what kills good deals is already diagnosed in this framework. The pitch wasn't weak. The forces were misread.

Field note: The habit force is consistently underestimated. Operators who reduce transition friction close more deals from existing owner dissatisfaction than operators who try to out-pitch the competition on features.
