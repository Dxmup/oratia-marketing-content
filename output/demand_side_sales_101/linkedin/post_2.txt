THE OWNER ACROSS FROM YOU HAS BEEN FRUSTRATED FOR 60 DAYS

They didn't call you because they woke up one morning and decided to shop for a new manager. There's a story that started weeks before your listing appointment. The appointment is the end of it, not the beginning.

Bob Moesta calls the pivotal event in that story the "struggling moment": the specific experience that made tolerating a bad situation finally unacceptable. Not general dissatisfaction, which the owner had been sitting with for months. The moment it crossed a line.

Many operators never find it. They ask "what's the problem with your current manager" and get generic complaints: slow maintenance, poor communication, underperforming revenue. These are symptoms. They're what the owner is willing to say in a first meeting.

The struggling moment is different. It's specific. It's usually emotional. And naming it is one of the highest-trust moves available in a listing appointment.

HOW TO FIND IT

One question: "What happened that made you finally decide to have this conversation?"

Then stop talking.

The answers sort into predictable categories: a guest issue the owner found out about from a review, not from their manager. A maintenance bill they didn't approve. A peak-season calendar gap they noticed while checking their own account. A co-owner conversation that went sideways.

WHY IT MATTERS

When you find the struggling moment and name it accurately, two things happen.

The owner feels understood in a way most managers haven't made them feel.

Your pitch becomes specific to their actual situation. The manager who pitches proactive communication to an owner whose struggling moment was a surprise review is speaking to the wound. Everyone else is pitching faster response times.

Script: "What happened that made you finally decide to have this conversation? The answer usually tells me more about what you need than anything else in this meeting."

Then: "That's exactly what we've built our process around. Let me show you how that would have gone differently with us."

MAPPING THE TIMELINE

After the struggling moment, go back further: "When did you first start noticing this was a problem?"

By the time you've mapped two or three events before the struggling moment, you know more about this owner than any manager who led with a deck.

Field note: The Skeptic's struggling moment is usually a trust violation. The Steward's is emotional: a guest review about the property they love. Identify the type and you know what to probe for.
