GETTING UP TO SPEED FASTER IS NOT A TALENT. IT IS A SKILL WITH A LEARNABLE METHOD.

The VRM operator entering a new market for the first time has two choices.

The first: start making calls, attend some local events, do enough listing appointments to gradually learn what the owners in this market care about, and accept the 6-12 month performance dip as the cost of entry.

The second: deliberately design the ramp.

Jill Konrath's core argument in Agile Selling is that the speed at which you get competent in a new selling environment is not fixed. It is a function of how deliberately you pursue the ramp. Elite operators don't just get better faster by accident. They manage the learning curve.

THE DELIBERATE RAMP

The deliberate ramp has four moves.

Define the minimum viable knowledge threshold. What do you need to know to have a credible first conversation with an owner in this market? Not everything. The minimum. For a new market, that might be: the three top-performing neighborhoods, the typical owner profile (investor vs. primary homeowner vs. out-of-market steward), the primary regulatory constraint, and the primary competitive alternative the owner is comparing you to. Five things. Get those five things in three days.

Talk to two operators who already work the market. Not to steal information. To understand the texture of owner conversations in this specific context. What objections are common here that aren't common in your home market? What does the Investor owner in a beach market care about differently than the Investor in a mountain market? These conversations compress 6 months of learning into two hours.

Build your owner type framework before the first appointments. Define the owner profile most common in the target segment. Write out the three diagnostic questions that open the best conversations with that profile. Practice them before you walk in the door.

Debrief after every conversation. What did the owner say that you didn't expect? What assumption was wrong? What would you ask differently in the next appointment? The operator who debriefs systematically improves faster than the one who just does more appointments.

Field note: The operators who ramp fastest in a new market are not necessarily the most knowledgeable. They're the most curious. The ones who ask more questions in early conversations and update their model faster based on what they hear. Curiosity is the mechanism. The framework is what directs it.
