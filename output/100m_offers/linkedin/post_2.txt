THE DREAM OUTCOME IS DIFFERENT FOR EVERY OWNER TYPE

Many VRM operators build one pitch and deliver it to every room. The language they use, "maximize your ROI," "protect your asset," "stress-free management," gets repeated across every appointment regardless of who's sitting across from them. The problem is that the dream outcome Hormozi describes as the first variable in the Value Equation is not a universal statement. It's a deeply personal one, and it varies by the type of owner you're sitting with.

Getting the dream outcome right is not a branding exercise. It's a diagnostic one.

THE SIX DREAM OUTCOMES IN VRM

Hormozi's framework pushes you to ask: what does this specific buyer actually want to achieve? Not what you assume they want. What they actually want. In VRM, the answer breaks along owner type lines.

THE INVESTOR: A property producing return on investment at or above their benchmark, with predictable monthly cash flow and minimal time overhead. "We'll maximize your ROI" lands. What lands harder is showing them a specific three-scenario forecast with conservative, realistic, and upside projections and telling them which one you'd bet on.

THE STEWARD: Their property cared for properly, guests having genuine experiences in a space that means something to them, and the financial return covering carrying costs while the asset appreciates. "We treat the home like our own" is the industry's most overused line. What a Steward actually needs to hear is that you understand why the home matters and can demonstrate it before you mention a number.

THE SKEPTIC: A management relationship that delivers what was promised, with no surprises and no feeling of being abandoned with problems after signing. The Skeptic's dream outcome isn't exceptional performance. It's a manager who doesn't disappear. Frame your offer around proof of follow-through, not projected upside.

THE DELEGATOR: Complete operational removal from the equation. Not "mostly hands-off." Not "we handle the day-to-day." Actually not thinking about it. The Delegator who is still fielding calls from their manager is not experiencing their dream outcome. Every element of your offer should signal total operational separation.

THE CONTROLLER: A management partner who executes well inside clearly understood boundaries and keeps them informed without requiring them to ask. The Controller doesn't want to give up control. They want a partner who respects the parameters and doesn't require constant oversight to stay inside them.

THE OPPORTUNIST: A property that pays for itself and requires less of them than they feared it would. The Opportunist's dream outcome is not aggressive. It's relief. They want to feel like the decision was simpler than they expected.

MATCHING LANGUAGE TO THE OUTCOME

The proposal that uses the same language for all six owner types leaves value on the table with five of them. "We'll maximize your ROI" closes Investors. "We'll make sure guests love your cabin the way you do" closes Stewards. The Delegator who hears ROI language from the first line is already calculating how much thinking they're going to have to do.

This is where the Value Equation's Dream Outcome variable does its real work. The question isn't "how do we describe our offer?" The question is "what outcome is this specific owner trying to achieve, and are we the most credible path to it?"

Script: "Before I walk you through what we do, I want to make sure I understand what success looks like for you specifically. For some owners it's about maximizing revenue. For others it's about protecting the property. And for others it's really just about being completely out of the operational picture. Which of those is closest to what you're actually solving for?"

This question resets the entire appointment. The owner tells you what outcome they're buying, and your proposal becomes the direct answer to what they said rather than a generic pitch hoping to land on something relevant.

Field note: The Delegator and Opportunist are the two owner types most likely to disengage if the first five minutes of the appointment are revenue-heavy. Both are solving for simplicity, not optimization. Open with questions about their current involvement before you introduce any performance data. The dream outcome surfaces in their answer, and it's almost never "more revenue."
