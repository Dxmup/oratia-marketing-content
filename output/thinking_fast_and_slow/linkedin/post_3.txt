THE BEST LISTING APPOINTMENT IN VRM CAN STILL LOSE TO A WEAK ENDING.

An operator I know runs exceptional listing appointments. Sharp opening, strong diagnostic, solid pitch. Owners consistently leave impressed.

She was also closing at about 40%.

When we talked through what was happening, the problem wasn't the middle of the appointment. It was the final ten minutes.

Daniel Kahneman's research on the peak-end rule is specific: people don't remember an experience as an average of all its moments. They remember the peak (the most emotionally intense point) and the end. The ninety minutes of solid pitch work is not what the owner takes home. The last ten minutes and the single most memorable moment are.

WHAT HAPPENS WHEN APPOINTMENTS TRAIL OFF

The typical listing appointment ending sounds like this:

"I'll get you the contract by Monday. Feel free to reach out if you have any questions. It was great meeting you."

The owner drives home and their spouse asks how it went. They describe a manager who seemed good, had solid numbers, and said they'd send something over.

Three days pass. The contract arrives. The owner reads it during the one hour of downtime they have on Saturday morning. Their spouse asks if they're sure. The owner says they think so.

Inertia wins.

WHAT A DESIGNED ENDING LOOKS LIKE

The peak moment and the ending are the two variables you can control.

The peak moment is the point in the appointment where the emotional intensity is highest. For most listing appointments, this is the transition removal offer: naming the specific friction that keeps owners from signing and removing it explicitly.

Script: "The thing that usually slows this down is the transition itself. Migrating your listing history, communicating with existing bookings, getting cleaners oriented to your property's standards. We handle all of that. If you decide to move forward, you don't manage the transition. We do."

Then close the appointment with a specific named next step, not a general intention.

Script: "I don't want to leave this open-ended. Can we put thirty minutes on the calendar for Thursday to go through any questions before the contract comes over? That way you're not reading it cold."

The owner who walks out with a named next step and a clear memory of the transition being removed is a different conversation than the owner who walks out with a vague intention to read the contract.

Field note: The peak moment is especially important with the Skeptic. Their fear is being abandoned after signing. Name that fear explicitly and remove it deliberately during the peak moment, not the contract, not the follow-up email.
