YOUR FEE ISN'T THE PROBLEM. YOUR SEQUENCING IS.

The commission objection in a VRM listing appointment is almost never actually about commission.

It's about what number the owner had in their head before you named yours.

Daniel Kahneman's research on anchoring is precise on this point: the first number introduced into a conversation disproportionately shapes every evaluation that follows. It doesn't matter if that number is relevant, accurate, or even from the current conversation. Once it's in the room, it's the reference point.

In VRM business development, this plays out in a specific and painful way.

An owner who paid 15% management fee to their last manager walks into your appointment anchored at 15%. If you charge 22%, you are not pitching value. You are defending a 7-point gap before you've said a single word about what you actually do.

HOW ANCHORING IS SET UP AGAINST YOU

Many operators open the fee conversation in response to the owner's question: "So what's your commission rate?"

The instinct is to answer directly, then justify. That instinct is expensive.

By answering the commission question first, you hand the anchor to whoever spoke last, which is the owner. Now you're negotiating downward from their reference point, not yours.

THE REFRAME

Before commission enters the conversation, anchor on what commission is a fraction of.

The sequence that works:

First, anchor on projected gross income. Run the number for the owner based on their specific property, market, and seasonal pattern. Make it concrete and specific to their situation, not a market average.

Second, surface what they're currently not capturing. Occupancy gaps, direct booking upside, maintenance cost gaps between what they're paying now and what a managed property typically pays. These aren't revenue projections; they're losses they're already incurring.

Third, name the transition cost they won't face. Address the friction of switching before they raise it.

Then commission comes up.

Script: "Our management fee is 22%. Based on the gross income projection we just built, that works out to roughly $X per year. The comparison you want to make isn't our fee versus your last manager's fee. It's our fee versus what you're currently leaving on the table."

Field note: Anchoring sequencing matters more with Investor owners than any other type. The Investor is doing math throughout the appointment. If they're doing the math from the wrong reference point, everything downstream is harder.
