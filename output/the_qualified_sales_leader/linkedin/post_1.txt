YOUR LISTING PIPELINE IS PROBABLY LYING TO YOU

Many VRM operators don't have a listing pipeline. They have a list of appointments they haven't followed up on.

The distinction matters. An appointment is an event. A pipeline opportunity is a qualified prospect with a specific pain, a confirmed decision-maker, a clear decision process, and a committed next step. Without those four elements, the name on your board is noise, not inventory.

John McMahon built MEDDIC: Metrics, Economic Buyer, Decision Criteria, Decision Process, Identify Pain, Champion. He developed it at PTC, one of the most disciplined enterprise sales organizations ever built. Applied to VRM listing pipelines, it produces one useful outcome: it forces you to tell the difference between an interested owner and a real opportunity.

WHAT MEDDIC LOOKS LIKE IN A LISTING APPOINTMENT

IDENTIFY PAIN

Not the surface complaint. The real one.

Script: "You mentioned self-management is getting to be a lot. Tell me more about that. What does a bad week actually look like for you?"

If the answer is "sometimes I have to answer messages on weekends," that's a preference. If the answer is "I got a maintenance call at 11 PM on my anniversary and my wife is done with the property," that is a pain. Qualified accordingly.

ECONOMIC BUYER

The person at the listing appointment is often not the person who makes the decision.

Script: "When you've made decisions like this in the past, bringing in outside help for something you were managing yourself, who else was part of that conversation?"

If the answer is "my husband handles the financial side of things," you've identified a second decision-maker you haven't met. Every follow-up before that meeting happens is aimed at the wrong person.

DECISION CRITERIA

Script: "If you were going to make a change, what would the right company need to look like? What would matter most to you?"

The owner who says "communication and a local team" has told you their criteria. The owner who says "I want to see my numbers every week and I don't want any surprises" has told you something more specific. Pitch to what they said, not what you assumed.

DECISION PROCESS

Script: "How do you normally make decisions like this? Is there anything that would need to happen before you'd feel comfortable moving forward?"

This question surfaces the internal dependencies the owner won't always volunteer: the spouse, the financial advisor, the attorney, the prior bad experience with another manager. Find out now, not after two weeks of silence.

Field note: Many operators skip these questions because they feel like an interrogation. They aren't. They're the difference between following up on a real opportunity and following up on hope.
