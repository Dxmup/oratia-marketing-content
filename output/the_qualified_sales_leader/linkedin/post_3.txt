YOUR REVENUE PROJECTION DOESN'T CLOSE LISTINGS. THE COST OF THE STATUS QUO DOES.

Many listing presentations lead with what the owner could earn. The owner sees a number that looks speculative because it's based on a future that hasn't happened yet.

The operator who leads with what the owner's current situation is already costing them has a different conversation. That number is real. It's already happening. It's verifiable.

McMahon calls this the Metrics element of MEDDIC: quantify the buyer's current pain in their own terms before you quantify the value of the solution. The contrast between the two is the argument.

THE BASELINE CALCULATION

Before presenting revenue projections, walk the owner through four questions:

1. What is your actual ADR right now, across all platforms?

Many self-managing owners don't know their blended rate. They know their Airbnb rate and their VRBO rate. They rarely know the weighted average across both, or how it compares to the competitive set. Pull the number. It's almost always lower than they think.

2. What is your occupancy relative to the market?

Self-managing owners often operate 15 to 25 occupancy points below professionally managed comparables in the same area. They book when inquiries come in. They don't run revenue management. The gap is usually visible without elaborate analysis.

3. How many peak-season nights are you blocking for personal use, and what is the opportunity cost of those blocks?

This is a sensitive question. Ask it carefully. The answer is almost always 10 to 20 nights during the highest-rate period of the year. At $350 to $600 a night, that's a real number on both sides of the ledger.

4. How many hours per week do you spend on the property right now?

Self-management is not free. The owner who spends 12 hours a week on guest messages, maintenance coordination, and listing management is running a part-time job they didn't sign up for. At any reasonable hourly rate, that's a cost of the status quo the owner has never calculated.

Now build the projection. The contrast between where they are and where they could be is the argument. The projection alone is a guess. The comparison is a case.

Field note: Owners who resist professional management often frame it as a trust issue. Beneath that framing, it is often a calculation problem: they haven't done the math on what self-management is costing them. The operator who does that math with them, in the room, changes the conversation from "should I trust this company" to "what is the cost of waiting."
