THE LISTING APPOINTMENT WENT WELL. IT ALWAYS DOES BEFORE IT GOES COLD.

You did a strong listing appointment. Good chemistry. The owner seemed ready. You followed up twice. Got "we're still discussing it." Three weeks later, the listing went to someone else.

This is not a pitch problem. It's a champion problem.

McMahon defines a champion as someone inside the buying organization who wants you to win and has the influence to move the deal forward. In enterprise sales, it's a senior contact who advocates for you in rooms you're not in. In VRM, it's the owner at the listing appointment, if they're actually the decision-maker.

Often they aren't.

THE HIDDEN DECISION-MAKER

VRM listing decisions are frequently more complex than they appear. The person at the appointment is often the interested party, not the deciding party.

The spouse who inherited the property and doesn't trust management companies after a bad experience six years ago. The business partner who controls the finances and needs to run the numbers. The adult child who set up the listing on Airbnb and feels territorial about changing the system. The financial advisor who told the owner to stay self-managed until the market softened.

None of these people were in the room. Any of them can kill the deal.

HOW TO SURFACE THIS BEFORE IT BECOMES A PROBLEM

Ask early. The best time to identify a hidden decision-maker is inside the listing appointment, not after it.

Script: "Before I pull together a proposal, I want to make sure I'm putting together the right information for everyone involved. When you've made decisions like this in the past, who else tends to be part of that conversation?"

If they name someone, the next question matters:

Script: "Would it make sense to include them in a follow-up call so they can ask questions directly? I find that tends to speed things up, and it gives them a chance to hear things from me rather than secondhand."

This is not a trick. It's an acknowledgment that the second decision-maker has questions you haven't answered yet, and that answering them in writing through a third party is the weakest possible format.

Field note: The owner who has a spouse or partner to consult isn't necessarily a difficult close. They're a close that requires an additional step. The operator who identifies that step in the appointment, rather than discovering it in the silence afterward, keeps control of the timeline.
