YOU ARE NOT THE HERO OF YOUR LISTING APPOINTMENT

Many VRM operators walk into a listing appointment and spend the first 10 minutes talking about their company. Their team. Their tech stack. Their occupancy numbers. Their management approach.

The owner sits there thinking: but what about me and my property?

Donald Miller's StoryBrand framework makes a simple argument: brands that position themselves as the hero fail. Brands that become the guide win. The guide helps the hero solve their problem. The guide doesn't need to be the star.

It maps onto listing appointments with uncomfortable precision.

THE WRONG FRAME

When an operator leads with credentials, they're auditioning for a job. The owner evaluates them against every other manager who has sat in that chair and made similar claims. The conversation becomes a feature comparison. The operator who claims the best occupancy rates beats the one who claims the best communication, until the next operator walks in and claims both.

This is a race that gets harder every year.

THE RIGHT FRAME

The operator who opens with questions learns something no credential slide ever reveals: what the owner is actually trying to accomplish and what the current situation has felt like for them.

"Tell me about the property. How long have you owned it? What originally made you want to put it into management?"

That's not small talk. That's reframing who the hero of this story is. The owner is the hero. Their property is what matters. Your job is to be the guide who helps them succeed.

Script: "I'd rather spend the first few minutes understanding your situation before I talk about how we work. What I say is going to mean a lot more if I know what you're actually dealing with."

That opener changes the dynamics of the next hour.

WHAT CHANGES WHEN YOU FLIP THE FRAME

The operator who starts with questions learns what the owner actually fears, not just what they complained about. They learn whether the problem is operational or emotional. They learn which of their capabilities are relevant to this owner's specific situation.

They also earn something no credential slide delivers: the sense that the owner has been heard.

Field note: Skeptic owners, the ones who have been burned before and walk in with their defenses up, are the most sensitive to hero-framing from the operator. An operator who opens by demonstrating they want to understand the owner's story disarms the Skeptic faster than any testimonial or occupancy chart.
