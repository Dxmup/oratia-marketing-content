SPECIFICITY IS THE SEPARATOR. MOST VRM MARKETING IGNORES IT.

Every VRM company in your market claims "hands-on service." None of them are closing owners with it.

Hopkins proved this in the 1920s. Not with theory, with data from split testing hundreds of ad campaigns. Specific, verifiable claims outperform impressive-sounding claims by a significant margin. The mechanism is straightforward: a precise claim is believable. A vague claim registers as noise.

The classic example is Schlitz beer. Hopkins helped Schlitz go from fifth place in the market to first. His discovery: Schlitz steam-cleaned their bottles at high temperature before filling them. So did every other brewery. Hopkins made it specific, put it in the copy, and Schlitz's sales separated from the field.

Not because they were doing something their competitors weren't. Because they described what they did specifically while their competitors spoke in generalities.

VRM marketing runs the same problem in the opposite direction. Every operator in every market claims:

"Hands-on service."
"Local expertise."
"We treat your home like our own."

These aren't differentiating claims. They are the baseline. Every competitor says the same words. When every operator makes the same claim, the claim loses all persuasive force.

THE REPLACEMENT FRAMEWORK

The fix is not a more creative tagline. The fix is replacing vague claims with specific verifiable statements.

Vague: "We drive better results for our owners."
Specific: "Our owners see their calendar fill an average of 11 days earlier than with their prior manager."

Vague: "We're responsive and easy to work with."
Specific: "Our average owner response time on maintenance requests is under four hours."

Vague: "We have deep knowledge of the local market."
Specific: "We've managed in this market for 11 years and have placed over 200 guests at properties within a quarter mile of yours."

If a claim can't be supported with a number, a timeline, a mechanism, or a before/after comparison, it's doing nothing. Pull it and replace it.

Field note: The Investor owner type runs every benefit claim through a mental filter: "How would they prove that?" Specific claims answer the question before it gets asked. Vague claims trigger the question with no answer waiting. The Investor leaves skeptical.
