When you lead with your constraints before an owner has a chance to find them, something shifts. They stop scanning your pitch for the catch. You've already offered one. And when your genuine strengths follow, they land with a credibility they never would have had if you'd led with them.

Todd Caponi's transparency framework starts with a counterintuitive discipline: build a real flaw inventory before you build your pitch. Not the flaws that make you sound humble. The ones that could actually cost you a deal if the owner found out on their own. The property types your model doesn't serve well. The operational gaps where you hand off to vendors. The owner behavior that creates friction your system wasn't built to absorb.

The newsletter this week covers how to construct the three-scenario revenue forecast with enough specificity that it holds up to an Investor's scrutiny, how the buyer-verified pipeline audit forces you to reclassify deals you've been carrying at inflated probability, and the connection between transparency at signing and retention at 12 months. The operators with the cleanest churn numbers are almost always the ones who sold conservative expectations and delivered against them.
