BUILDING A FIFTEEN-MINUTE QUALIFICATION PROTOCOL

Khalsa and Illig's core argument is that the best sales conversations are mutual evaluations. The operator is evaluating the owner as rigorously as the owner is evaluating the operator. Both parties benefit from a clear, early no.

Most VRM operators don't have a qualification framework. They have a list of questions they ask to understand the property and the owner's goals, but no structured criteria for what makes an owner a good fit versus a bad fit. Without criteria, every listing appointment runs long and every ambiguous signal gets the benefit of the doubt.

Here's a working qualification framework for VRM:

QUALIFICATION CRITERIA (SIGNALS OF A GOOD FIT)

Owner is clear on what they want and can describe it without contradicting themselves. Self-managing owners who say "I just want it off my plate" are describing a full handoff. That's workable. Owners who say "I want full management but I also want to approve every guest" are describing something that isn't.

Owner has realistic expectations about revenue. Any expectation that assumes year-round occupancy at peak-season rates without accepting any owner-blocked dates needs to be addressed before signing. It won't get better after signing.

Owner demonstrates self-awareness about their prior manager relationships. "We weren't a good fit" is different from "they were terrible." The former shows the owner can evaluate their own role in a relationship. The latter doesn't.

Owner's property is in a condition and location that can deliver what they're expecting. This is operational, not personal, but it matters. A property that needs $15,000 in updates to compete at the rate point the owner expects is a structural problem that needs to be named before the contract is signed.

Owner is making the decision themselves, or the decision-making unit is fully represented. The listing appointment that requires a follow-up with "the person who actually decides" is a sign that you're pitching the wrong person.

DISQUALIFICATION SIGNALS

These don't automatically disqualify a listing, but each warrants a direct conversation before moving to a proposal:

More than two prior managers in three years. Not always disqualifying, but the pattern deserves investigation. "What happened with each of them?" is worth asking.

Wants to be involved in day-to-day decisions. Approval on guest communications, maintenance decisions, pricing changes. This is co-management, not management. Some operators offer it. Most don't. Know which you are before the conversation continues.

Revenue expectation that the property can't support. Run the numbers honestly before you leave the appointment. Don't send a proposal that shows the number they want and plan to revise it downward after signing.

Current relationship conflict. The owner who is in an active dispute with their current manager, actively hostile, or midway through a legal process is bringing complexity you should understand before getting involved.

Family or partnership dynamics that aren't resolved. "I need to talk to my partner" is normal. "I need to convince my partner" is a different conversation.

THE COMMITMENT TEST IN FULL

Post 3 gave the short version of the commitment test. Here's the full sequence:

Before wrapping up: "Before we finish, I want to make sure I understand where you are in the decision process. We've covered [main topics from the appointment]. Do you have any questions or concerns we didn't get to?"

After that is resolved: "Assuming our proposal looks right to you and we've addressed everything we talked about today, what does your decision process look like from here?"

If the answer is specific (involves named people, named criteria, named timeline), it's real. If the answer is vague ("I'd need to think about it"), follow up directly: "Is there something specific you're uncertain about that would help to address now rather than after the proposal?"

The goal is not to pressure. The goal is to end the appointment with either a clear path forward or a clear understanding of what's in the way.
