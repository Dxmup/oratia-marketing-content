"LET ME THINK ABOUT IT" IS NOT A PIPELINE ENTRY

Most VRM operators leave the listing appointment reading the owner's engagement as a buying signal. The owner asked good questions. Said the proposal sounded promising.

Khalsa and Illig call this the illusion of commitment. The owner wasn't committing. They were being polite. Polite owners ghost.

The listing appointment creates social pressure to agree. "I'll think about it" is the most comfortable way to end without committing. By the time the operator follows up, the social pressure is gone and so is the urgency.

THE COMMITMENT TEST

The fix is simple but uncomfortable to run: before the appointment ends, ask a question that requires the owner to describe a specific next step, not their interest level.

Script: "Assuming our proposal looks right to you and we've covered the main concerns, what would your decision process look like from here?"

This question separates genuine commitment signals from social courtesy:

A genuine commitment signal: "I'd need to talk it over with my co-owner and then we'd be ready to move forward if the numbers check out." The owner has told you who the decision-making unit is, what they need to resolve it, and that they're willing to move.

A politeness signal: "I just need some time to process and think it through." The owner hasn't committed to any decision process. They've told you they're not ready to close the loop.

The point isn't to pressure the owner. It's to surface the real objection before they leave the room, while you're still in a position to address it.

WHAT HAPPENS WHEN YOU SKIP THIS

You send the proposal. Follow up two days later. No response. Try again three days after that. Still nothing. Mark it "soft no" and move on.

The owner wasn't a soft no. They had a question that felt too awkward to ask in the room. The commitment test would have surfaced it.

Field note: Delegator owners who appear highly enthusiastic are particularly prone to illusory commitment. Their enthusiasm is genuine, but they often have a family member or financial partner who is skeptical and whose approval they haven't secured. Leaving the appointment without the commitment test means finding out via silence.

A well-run listing appointment ends with clarity, not hope.
