THE OWNER HAS FOUR CONCERNS. THEY'RE NOT GOING TO TELL YOU WHAT THEY ARE.

Most VRM owners come to a listing appointment with three to five specific concerns. They will not bring them up. They'll nod along, take your proposal, and ghost.

Khalsa and Illig's framework for this is direct: put all the issues on the table before the owner does. This is not a technique. It's an acknowledgment that you know how this conversation usually goes.

WHAT THIS SOUNDS LIKE IN THE ROOM

Script: "Before I walk you through what we do, I want to cover the things most owners are thinking about when they're considering switching managers. The most common ones: what happens to existing bookings during transition, whether they'll lose revenue from direct reservations, what happens if they're unhappy in the first 90 days, and what the full fee picture actually looks like. Are those on your list? Is there anything else you want to make sure we cover?"

This opener does three things. It signals you understand what owners actually worry about. It gives the owner permission to say what they were going to hold back. And it tells them you're not going to avoid the hard topics.

THE OWNER TYPES AND THEIR HIDDEN CONCERNS

The Skeptic is worried about being misled again. Their concern is rarely about the fee. It's "how do I know you won't disappear after I sign?" Name it: "Some owners who've had bad experiences are specifically worried about what communication looks like once you're no longer a prospect. Worth talking about?"

The Controller is worried about losing oversight. Their concern isn't revenue. It's "will I still know what's happening with my property?" Name it: "Owners who like to stay involved often find the transition to full management harder than expected. Let's talk about that upfront."

The Investor is worried about inflated projections. Their concern is "what's the real number once the fees come out?" Name it: "I want to give you a realistic range rather than a best-case number."

Field note: The owner who adds a fifth concern when you ask "is there anything else?" is taking the appointment seriously. The one who says "no, those cover it" is still processing. Give them a moment.

Naming the hard things early doesn't scare owners away. It earns their candor.
