THE VRM LISTING YOU SHOULDN'T HAVE SIGNED

Most VRM operators measure their listing appointments by one outcome: did the owner sign?

Khalsa and Illig have a different measure. The right question isn't whether you got the listing. It's whether the listing should have been signed at all.

THE MATH ON A BAD-FIT LISTING

An owner who wants to be consulted before any maintenance over $100. An owner whose last three managers "didn't work out." An owner whose revenue expectations the property can't support.

These are disqualification signals. An operator with a real qualification framework surfaces them in the first fifteen minutes. An operator without one signs all three and spends the next year managing the consequences.

The full cost of a bad-fit listing isn't just management time. It's the complaints, the reviews, the owner who tells three others in their network not to use you. The listing that looked like $8,000 per year in management fees ends up costing $25,000 in operational drag.

WHAT MUTUAL QUALIFICATION LOOKS LIKE IN PRACTICE

The shift is simple: instead of asking "how do I convince this owner to sign?" ask "is this owner actually a good fit for how we operate?"

Three questions worth asking early:

"Tell me about your experience with your current or prior manager. What worked well? What didn't?" The answer tells you almost everything. An owner who describes every prior manager only in terms of what they did wrong is worth scrutinizing.

"What does your ideal management relationship look like day-to-day?" An owner who wants weekly calls, approval rights on guest selection, and input on pricing strategy is describing co-management.

"What are your revenue expectations for year one?" If the answer isn't achievable, that gap needs to be on the table before the contract is signed.

Field note: Investor owners with strong revenue expectations and a history of switching managers are almost always a disqualification risk. They're not changing managers because the managers were bad. They're changing managers because their expectations are disconnected from what the property can deliver.

Signing fewer listings isn't the goal. Signing the right ones is.
