WHEN AN OWNER SAYS "I WANT BETTER COMMUNICATION," YOU'VE LEARNED ALMOST NOTHING

Every owner who has ever been disappointed by a property manager says they want better communication. It's not wrong. But it's also not useful.

"Better communication" covers a range that goes from "I want weekly revenue updates" to "I want someone to call me immediately if a guest leaves a one-star review" to "I drove 45 minutes at 2 AM because my manager didn't pick up and a guest was locked out." Those are three different pain levels. They require three different solutions. And they will produce three different levels of urgency when you follow up.

The general answer tells you the general preference. The specific incident tells you the depth.

ASK ABOUT THE INCIDENT, NOT THE PREFERENCE

Fitzpatrick's principle is simple: ask about the past, not the future. Ask about specific events, not general opinions. Future preferences are almost always optimistic. Past behavior is real.

Instead of "How important is communication to you?" try "Tell me about a specific time when communication with your manager broke down. What happened?"

The owner who describes a situation that cost them a five-star review, that required them to personally intervene during peak season, that left a guest frustrated and the owner embarrassed: that owner is not the same prospect as the one who says "yeah I'd like more check-ins." The first one has a problem with urgency. The second one has a preference without urgency.

You cannot price and qualify the same way.

SPECIFICS CLOSE THE GAP

When you understand the specific incident, you can build the specific response.

Script: "Based on what you've described, the maintenance delay in July and the guest complaint you had to handle yourself, here's exactly how we would handle that differently. [Specific process, specific contact protocol, specific accountability structure.] That situation would not have happened this way."

That is a different conversation than "We have great communication." And it is only possible if you asked the right question first.

The operator who does generic discovery loses to the operator who has specific knowledge of the owner's pain. Specifics create authority. Authority closes the appointment.
