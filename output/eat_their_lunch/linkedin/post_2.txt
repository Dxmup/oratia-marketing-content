HOW TO RUN A COMPETITIVE LISTING APPOINTMENT WITHOUT LOSING THE ROOM

There's a specific mistake operators make when they know they're pitching against an incumbent.

They attack.

They bring up the competitor's reviews. They mention the fee dispute they heard about secondhand. They imply the owner has been underserved without quite saying it.

And it almost always backfires.

When you make the owner wrong for their prior decision, they stop evaluating you. They start defending the choice they already made. The psychological cost of admitting a mistake is higher than the cost of tolerating a mediocre manager, and you've just triggered it.

Iannarino's competitive displacement framework in Eat Their Lunch is built around a different sequence: acknowledge what the incumbent has done, identify the specific gap, and position yourself as the incremental upgrade rather than the complete indictment.

THE STRUCTURE OF A DISPLACEMENT CONVERSATION

Step 1: Acknowledge the existing relationship.

Script: "I'm sure there are things about how they've managed the property that have worked well for you. I want to understand what those are, because I don't want to pitch you on something you already have."

This signals you're not here to make them feel foolish. It asks them to articulate what they actually value, which tells you where the bar is. And it creates space for them to name what hasn't worked, on their own terms.

Step 2: Surface the gap without naming the competitor.

Script: "Based on what you've described, the piece that's missing sounds like [specific thing]. Is that fair?"

Let them confirm or correct it. You want them owning the problem, not you diagnosing it for them.

Step 3: Show how you solve for that specific thing. Not your full service offering. The specific gap they just named.

Script: "That's actually where we're different. Here's exactly how we handle [specific thing] and what that looks like for you."

THE COMMON ERROR IN STEP 3

Many operators get to step 3 and pivot back to a full-feature pitch. They've just had a productive diagnostic conversation, and they abandon it to run their canned presentation.

Stay in the gap. The owner just told you what the decision is about. Solve for that, and get out.

Field note: Steward owners are especially sensitive to feeling judged for prior decisions. The acknowledgment step is not optional with them. Investor owners will move faster if you get to the financial gap quickly, but you still need the acknowledgment to establish that you're listening, not pitching.
