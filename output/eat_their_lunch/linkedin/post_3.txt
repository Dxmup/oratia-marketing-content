LOSING THE FIRST CONVERSATION DOES NOT MEAN LOSING THE OWNER

Many VRM operators write off a prospect the moment they hear "I'm going to stay with my current manager for now."

That's the wrong response.

Most owners who eventually switch managers don't do it the first time someone makes a case for it. They do it when the pain crosses a threshold. A guest incident that goes badly. A missed booking during peak season. A maintenance issue that sits unresolved for two weeks. A property statement that doesn't add up.

The operator who is still present when that moment arrives gets the call. The one who moved on after the first no gets nothing.

Iannarino's frame in Eat Their Lunch applies directly here: the prospect who is locked into a competitor is not a lost opportunity. They are a future deal on a longer timeline. The question is whether you have a system for staying in front of them until the window opens.

THE NURTURE TRACK MOST OPERATORS DON'T HAVE

A structured follow-up sequence for locked-in owners looks nothing like a sales cadence. No "just checking in" emails. No feature updates nobody asked for.

What actually works:

A market update 60-90 days after the initial conversation. Not a pitch. A one-page analysis of occupancy rates and ADR for comparable properties in their market. Something specific to their ZIP code or neighborhood. Useful on its own, completely separate from whether they switch managers.

A property-specific observation 4-6 months out. If you had a meaningful conversation with the owner, you know something about their property. Maybe there's a platform gap you noticed. A pricing strategy improvement. A review response issue. Send a specific observation with no ask attached.

A check-in at the 12-month mark. Not a formal pitch. A brief message: "I know we talked about a year ago. Wanted to reconnect and see where things stand."

Most VRM operators do none of this. The ones who build it see a conversion rate from "no for now" to "yes" that makes the nurture investment obvious.

The owner's situation changes. Your job is to be the operator they think of when it does.

Field note: This track works especially well with Investor and Opportunist owner types, who tend to re-evaluate management relationships based on financial performance benchmarks. A market comp showing their property underperforming peers in the same neighborhood is often the trigger. Make sure you're the one who sends it.
