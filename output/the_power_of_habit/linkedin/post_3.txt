YOU DON'T NEED TO REBUILD YOUR ENTIRE BD PROCESS. YOU NEED ONE KEYSTONE HABIT.

The operators I've watched fail at building a BD function have something in common. They try to change everything at once. New CRM. New outreach scripts. New follow-up cadence. New comp structure. New reporting. All of it launched inside the same 30-day window, all of it competing for attention, all of it abandoned by week six.

Duhigg's research on keystone habits explains what works better.

Some habits have outsized downstream effects. When a keystone habit is established, it creates structures and small wins that cascade into other behaviors without direct effort. You don't force the adjacent behaviors. They follow.

In VRM owner acquisition, the keystone is usually the weekly pipeline review.

WHAT THE PIPELINE REVIEW ACTUALLY DOES

A 30-minute weekly review forces you to categorize every deal. Who is warm. Who is cold. Who needs a call this week. Who has stalled and why. That categorization triggers outreach. The outreach produces conversations. The conversations produce pipeline movement. The movement produces a visible win that reinforces the habit of doing the review next week.

Operators who skip the review default to chasing whoever emailed most recently. Their outreach is reactive, not systematic. The week ends with activity and no pipeline advancement.

Operators who protect the review get a different outcome. The review is the keystone. Everything else follows from it.

THE PROTECTION QUESTION

The question isn't whether to do the weekly review. It's whether you'll protect it when operations gets loud. And operations will get loud. A guest will have a problem. A cleaning crew will fall through. An owner will need attention.

The keystone habit survives only if it's treated as a standing appointment, not a nice-to-have. The 30 minutes is not negotiable. The pipeline won't move itself.

Field note: We see this pattern in the operators building on Oratia. The ones who run a consistent weekly review are the ones whose pipelines actually move. The review isn't magic. It's just the one habit that forces all the others.
