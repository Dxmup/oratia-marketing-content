Post 1 of 3

YOU DIDN'T LOSE TO A COMPETITOR. YOU LOST TO NO DECISION.

Many VRM operators lose their best deals to a competitor that doesn't exist.

The appointment goes well. The owner likes the revenue projection. Everything feels aligned. Then the owner says "let me think about it" and ghosts. The operator assumes they lost to the discount manager down the street.

They didn't. They lost to the status quo.

Research in The Jolt Effect shows that 40 to 60 percent of complex B2B deals end in no decision. The buyer isn't driven by FOMO (Fear Of Missing Out). They're paralyzed by FOMU (Fear Of Messing Up).

WHAT FOMU LOOKS LIKE IN VRM

When a vacation rental owner hesitates, it's rarely because your fee is 2% too high. It's because switching property managers feels like a massive, risky undertaking. Think about what they're imagining:

- Breaking a contract with their current manager, possibly awkwardly.
- Migrating listings, reviews, and existing bookings without anything breaking.
- Telling their spouse they made the switch, and then being wrong about it.
- Reliving all the logistical friction they went through when they signed with the last manager.

If you respond to that mental load by talking louder about your dynamic pricing engine, you're answering the wrong question. You're selling upside when the owner is drowning in risk.

THE FOMU DIAGNOSTIC

Before the appointment ends, test whether you're losing to a competitor or to inertia:

Script: "If you weren't working with your current manager at all, starting completely fresh today, would this be a straightforward decision for you? I'm trying to understand whether your hesitation is about us versus someone else, or whether it's really about the hassle of switching at all."

If they say "I'd still want to compare a few options," you're in a competitive deal. If they say "Probably yeah, it's really just the switch that feels overwhelming," you're losing to inertia. The playbook is completely different. Stop selling upside and start de-risking the transition.

Field note: The Opportunist owner type is among the most FOMU-prone of the six. They came in curious, not committed, and inertia is their natural default. These deals close when the transition feels effortless, not when the revenue case gets more compelling.
