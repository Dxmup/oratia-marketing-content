YOU DON'T WIN A LISTING APPOINTMENT WITH A BETTER ANSWER. YOU WIN IT WITH A BETTER QUESTION.

Most VRM operators think discovery questions are for gathering information.

Cialdini's research on pre-suasion suggests they're doing twice the work. The right question before a pitch doesn't just surface what the owner is thinking. It directs what the owner is thinking.

Cialdini calls it the focusing question. Where attention goes, importance follows. And the question is what directs the attention.

THE GAP BETWEEN GENERIC AND PRE-SUASIVE

A generic discovery question: "Tell me a little about the property and how things are going."

The owner's attention goes everywhere: recent bookings, the guest who left a bad review last month, their new couch, the management fee they're hoping to negotiate.

A pre-suasive focusing question: "Before I walk you through what we do, I want to understand what's been the most time-consuming part of managing the property yourself. Not just the revenue side, but what actually takes up your evenings and weekends."

The owner's attention goes to operational pain. Specifically. Every answer they give is a problem you're about to solve.

Same intent. Completely different frame for everything that follows.

THE QUESTIONS THAT SET THE FRAME

A few focusing questions that consistently channel owner attention toward solvable pain:

"What's the one thing about managing the property that you most wish wasn't your problem?"

"When something goes wrong with a guest, what does that actually look like for you on a practical level?"

"If you handed this property to a manager and never had to think about operations again, what would change about your week?"

These aren't tricks. They're honest questions that surface real pain. The pre-suasive effect is a byproduct of the specificity, not the intent.

The owner who answers any of those questions has now made their problem focal. The pitch that follows is evaluated through the lens of whether it solves the problem they just named.

Field note: Skeptic owners respond particularly well to questions that invite them to voice their concerns before you've said anything about your company. The Skeptic's default posture is defensive. A focusing question that gives them permission to say what they're worried about shifts them from defensive to disclosive. Once they've told you what they're afraid of, you can address it directly and specifically.
