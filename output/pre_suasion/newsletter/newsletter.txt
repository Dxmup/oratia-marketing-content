THE THING ABOUT INFLUENCE THAT MOST SALES TRAINING MISSES

Most sales training treats the pitch as the unit of influence. Prepare better. Deliver better. Handle objections better.

Cialdini's Pre-Suasion is built on a different observation: the moment before the pitch is as influential as the pitch itself, and often more so. What you arrange the buyer to be thinking and feeling immediately before your message arrives determines how that message lands.

In VRM, this translates directly. The owner who walks into a listing appointment thinking "I'm here to evaluate a vendor" is in a fundamentally different decision mode than the owner who walks in thinking "I'm here to solve a problem I've already been thinking about." The operator who sets that frame before the pitch begins is operating with a structural advantage.

The posts this week covered three mechanisms. The newsletter builds the full sequence.

THE PRE-APPOINTMENT TOUCH

Pre-suasion begins before you walk in the door.

Most VRM operators send either nothing before a listing appointment or a generic "looking forward to meeting with you" confirmation. This is a missed opportunity.

The pre-appointment touch is a small act of genuine value delivered before the meeting with no pitch attached.

This might be a brief, property-specific market note: "I was pulling data on the comparable properties in your area before our meeting. Thought you might find it useful to see where your property sits against the managed comps. Sending it over now. Happy to walk through it when we meet."

It might be a specific observation about their current listing if it's public: an occupancy gap, a review trend, a pricing pattern that's costing them.

This serves two functions. First, it creates genuine reciprocity before the appointment, not during or after it. Cialdini's research shows that uninvited, unexpected value creates the strongest reciprocity response. Second, it primes the owner's attention toward a specific problem before you've said anything in person.

The owner who received something useful before the meeting shows up differently. The conversation starts from a different place.

THE ARRIVAL EXCHANGE

The first two minutes are not setup. They are the highest-leverage two minutes in the appointment.

What you say walking from the car to the door, or before you sit down, primes the owner's attention for everything that follows. This is the privileged moment. It runs out fast.

The arrival exchange should channel the owner's attention toward the problem you're built to solve, using language specific to their situation.

Market data frame: "I was looking at the self-managed properties in your area this morning. A few of them are sitting at 58% occupancy right now. That's about 15 points below what a well-managed property typically runs in this market. That's the gap we spend most of our time closing."

Operational pain frame: "Before we get into what we do, I want to make sure I understand what's actually costing you the most right now, not just in revenue but in time and attention. That's the problem I want to make sure we actually solve for you."

The property walk frame: Make a specific observation about the property itself. Not a compliment. An observation. "I noticed your hot tub hasn't been reviewed in the last six guest stays. That usually means it's not being featured in the listing photos or the guest communication, which is a revenue gap on a property like this."

Each of these is honest and useful. Each also channels attention toward a specific problem before the pitch begins.

THE FOCUSING QUESTION

After the arrival exchange comes the first formal question. This is where frame-setting either locks in or dissipates.

Generic: "Tell me a little about the property and how things are going."

Pre-suasive:

"What's the most time-consuming part of managing the property yourself, not just financially but operationally?"

"When something goes wrong at the property, what does that actually look like for your week?"

"If you handed this property off to a manager today and didn't have to think about operations anymore, what's the first thing you'd stop dealing with?"

The owner who answers any of these questions has made their problem focal. Every answer surfaces a specific pain that your service can address. The pitch that follows is evaluated through that lens.

THE FOCAL DIMENSION

Before you arrive at an appointment, decide on one dimension to lead with. The dimension you make focal first is the one the owner uses to compare you against every other option they consider.

Pick the dimension where you have the clearest genuine advantage and make it the opening frame.

This is not about hiding your other strengths. It's about controlling the decision surface. You can discuss everything you do. But the thing you say first, specifically and deliberately, is the thing the owner carries into their evaluation.

At the end of the appointment, return to it explicitly:

Script: "We've covered a lot today. When you're comparing your options, I'd want you to come back to how we handle owner communication specifically. That's where we've invested the most and where I think you'll see the biggest difference. If it would be useful, I can connect you with two or three owners who can speak to that directly."

UNITY AND SHARED EXPERIENCE

Cialdini introduces a seventh principle of influence in Pre-Suasion: Unity. It's distinct from liking. It's about the sense of shared identity.

Operators who have personally managed a vacation rental property, or who have gone through the experience of handing off a property and worrying about whether the manager would actually care about it, have genuine shared experience with the owners they pitch.

Using that experience correctly is not about performing relatability. It's about naming the specific moment of shared recognition accurately.

"I've managed my own properties. The hardest part of handing them off was not the revenue question. It was not knowing if the manager would handle a guest problem at 11 PM the way I would. That's the part I built our process around."

That is Unity. Not "I understand what you're going through." The specific named experience that says: I have been in that room.
