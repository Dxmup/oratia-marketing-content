THE OWNER WHO ASKS ABOUT YOUR COMMISSION RATE FIRST HAS ALREADY CATEGORIZED YOU

That first question is not a negotiating tactic. It's a signal.

When an owner opens a listing appointment by asking about your commission rate, they have already categorized you as a commodity: one in a series of interchangeable property managers they are shopping on price. Everything that follows in that conversation is evaluated through that frame.

April Dunford's Obviously Awesome is a positioning book, not a sales book. But it addresses this problem directly. Her core argument: positioning happens whether you design it or not. Customers categorize your product the moment they encounter you. If you don't establish your position explicitly, they use whatever context clues are available and arrive at the default. For most VRM operators, the default is "property manager," which is a commodity category.

THE CATEGORY SETS THE CRITERIA

This is Dunford's sharpest insight for operators: whichever market category an owner places you in determines the criteria they use to evaluate you.

Property manager category criteria: commission rate, occupancy guarantee, response time, reviews.

These criteria are not unique to you. They are set by the category. Every management company is being evaluated on the same dimensions because they all accepted the same category.

Luxury vacation rental management for coastal properties: criteria shift. Now the owner is asking about your specific market expertise, your direct booking capability, your communication protocols for high-end guests, and your experience with properties at this price point.

The features did not change. The frame did. And the frame changed what questions get asked.

WHAT CATEGORY SHIFT LOOKS LIKE IN PRACTICE

Before Dunford: "We're a full-service vacation rental management company offering professional photography, dynamic pricing, and a 24/7 maintenance line."

The owner's mental response: I've heard this before. What's the commission?

After Dunford: "We work specifically with owners of luxury properties in this coastal market who want a management relationship where they hear about problems after they're solved, not while they're happening. Our operation is built around that outcome."

The owner's mental response: Is this the right fit for my situation?

That is a different conversation. The second opener prompts a qualification question, not a price question. The owner is evaluating fit, not comparing features.
