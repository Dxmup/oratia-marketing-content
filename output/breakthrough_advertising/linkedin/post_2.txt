NOT EVERY OWNER WHO AGREES TO MEET WITH YOU IS IN THE SAME PLACE

An owner says yes to the conversation. You show up with the same presentation you give everyone: platform overview, revenue projections, management fee, communication promise.

Sometimes it lands. Sometimes the owner nods through the whole thing and says they'll think about it.

The reason is usually not the pitch. It's that the pitch was designed for a different owner than the one in the room.

Schwartz called this the Awareness Ladder. Buyers don't arrive from the same starting position. Some are barely aware they have a problem. Some have been thinking about switching for six months and want to evaluate their final two options. Delivering the same pitch to both misses one of them entirely.

WHERE OWNERS TEND TO LAND

Most listing appointments involve owners at one of three places:

Stage 1: Curious but not convinced the switch is worth the disruption. They know professional management exists. They haven't decided it's right for them.

Stage 2: They've decided to switch and are interviewing managers. They believe in professional management. They're evaluating who.

Stage 3: They had a bad experience with a prior manager and are deciding whether to try again. They have a specific list of concerns that need addressing before they'll commit.

The same pitch serves none of these owners equally well.

THE DIAGNOSTIC

Ask in the first five minutes: "What prompted you to look at your management options now?"

The answer tells you almost everything. "I've been self-managing for two seasons and it's getting to be too much" is Stage 1 moving toward Stage 2. "My neighbor told me I should talk to someone" is early Stage 1. "I just ended my contract with another manager" is Stage 3.

Stage 1 owner: Start with the problem, not the solution. Help them articulate what isn't working before you pitch what you offer.

Stage 2 owner: Skip the education. Go straight to the mechanism. They know why professional management matters. They want to know what makes you different.

Stage 3 owner: Address the prior experience first. Don't pitch over it.

Field note: Controllers and Skeptics often arrive at Stage 3 not because of a prior bad manager but because they've been self-managing and loss of control is the concern they're walking in with. The diagnostic question reveals which version you're dealing with.
