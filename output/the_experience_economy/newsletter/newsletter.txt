This week covered three posts from The Experience Economy by B. Joseph Pine II and James H. Gilmore. The common thread: most VRM operators are delivering professional services in an economy that has moved past services. The gap between where the industry is and where it needs to be is not a technology gap or a marketing gap. It is an experience design gap.

Post 1 made the case that commoditization is not a market condition. It is the result of competing at the service tier when the experience tier is available. Post 2 applied Pine and Gilmore's staging concept to the listing appointment: what owners remember, what negative cues undermine even strong pitches, and one question that changes the dynamic. Post 3 introduced the owner experience audit: how to identify and eliminate the negative touchpoints in the first 90 days before adding any new positive ones.

This newsletter goes further. It takes Pine and Gilmore's full value progression, from commodities through goods, services, and experiences to transformation, and maps it onto the VRM owner relationship across acquisition, onboarding, and retention.

---

THE FULL PROGRESSION AND WHERE MOST VRM OPERATORS STOP

Pine and Gilmore describe five tiers of economic value:

Commodities: raw materials, interchangeable, priced by the market.

Goods: manufactured products with consistent specifications.

Services: activities performed on behalf of a client. This is where most VRM companies compete.

Experiences: events that engage the client personally and are memorable.

Transformations: outcomes that change who the client is. The highest-value tier.

Most VRM operators stop at services. They define their offer in terms of activities: we handle guest communication, maintenance coordination, listing optimization, revenue management. These are service descriptions. They are accurate. They are not differentiated.

The operators with the lowest churn and the highest referral rates have, often without naming it this way, moved into the experience tier and, in some cases, begun doing transformational work with their owners.

---

THE ACQUISITION EXPERIENCE

Too many listing appointments are service pitches. The operator walks through what they do, shows a revenue projection, names the fee, and waits for a response. The owner has heard this from two or three managers before. The appointment ends in "let me think about it" not because the operator failed to deliver information but because they failed to stage an experience.

What does a listing appointment look like when it is designed as an experience?

It starts before the owner arrives. A brief pre-appointment note that tells the owner what to expect, what to prepare, and one question to think about: "What would need to be true six months in for this to have been the right decision?" This is not a logistical note. It is a cue that signals this is a different kind of conversation.

The appointment opens with a question, not a presentation. The operator who asks "What have you left every prior conversation still wanting to know?" signals something none of the others have: that this appointment is about the owner's specific situation, not a templated pitch.

The proposal does not lead with the fee. It opens with a brief observation about the specific property: one thing the operator noticed that no one else mentioned. This shows preparation. It also shows that the operator sees the property the way the owner sees it.

The leave-behind is specific. Not a company brochure. A one-page document showing three things the operator would do with this specific listing in the first 60 days, with a rationale for each.

These are not expensive additions. They are designed moments. Each one communicates: you are in capable hands and we've done this before. Collectively, they create a listing appointment that is genuinely memorable.

THE ONBOARDING EXPERIENCE

Onboarding is the highest-risk period in the owner relationship. The owner has just made a decision, expectations are at their peak, and the first few touchpoints either confirm or undermine everything they were promised.

Pine and Gilmore's negative cue principle is most important here. The onboarding experience is only as strong as its worst moment in the first 30 days.

THE CRITICAL TOUCHPOINTS IN THE FIRST 30 DAYS

The contract and paperwork process. Most operators send a DocuSign link. This is a neutral at best, a negative cue if the owner has any confusion about what they're signing. The fix: a three-sentence cover note with every document. What this is, what to do if something looks wrong, and who to call. This is three sentences that convert a neutral into a positive.

The first guest communication handoff. The owner wants to know that their property is going to guests the way they intended. A brief note after the first booking, confirming the guest profile and any specific note about property prep, closes the anxiety loop.

The first maintenance event. Whether it is minor or significant, the first maintenance event is a test. The owner is watching how it is handled, how quickly they are informed, and whether the loop is closed. A maintenance communication protocol that requires a resolution update before 10 PM on any open issue eliminates the most common first-30-day negative cue.

The first revenue statement. This arrives on the 15th and most operators send a spreadsheet with no narrative context. For a new owner who has never seen a VRM revenue statement, this is confusing and mildly alarming. A three-paragraph cover note explaining what the statement shows, how performance compared to projection, and what the next 30 days look like converts a neutral into a signal of competence.

THE TRANSFORMATION TIER

Pine and Gilmore's highest tier of economic value is transformation: the offering where the provider's success is measured not by what they deliver but by who the customer becomes.

For VRM operators, this is mostly unexplored territory. The services tier asks: did we handle the maintenance request? The experience tier asks: does the owner feel that working with us is different from working with anyone else? The transformation tier asks: is the owner a better property investor because of this relationship?

This matters for retention because owners who feel transformed by the relationship are genuinely difficult to poach. They are not evaluating the relationship based on fees or software. They are evaluating it based on whether this manager has helped them make better decisions about a major financial asset.

WHAT TRANSFORMATION LOOKS LIKE IN PRACTICE

The annual performance review. Most VRM operators send a revenue summary. Transformation-oriented operators go further: where does this property sit relative to comparable properties in the market, what one renovation would have the highest ADR impact, what would a pricing strategy shift do to Q4 occupancy. This is advisory work. It changes how the owner thinks about the property.

The renovation conversation. When the owner asks whether they should replace the HVAC or repaint the exterior first, the transformation-oriented operator has a point of view grounded in market data: here is what guests mention most in reviews, here is what comparable listings have done that moved their rating, here is what I would do in your position. This is not just property management. It is an advisory relationship.

The investment question. A small number of owners, Investors and Opportunists in particular, will eventually ask whether their property is still the right use of their capital or whether they should be looking at different assets. The VRM manager who can engage with that question seriously, and refer to trusted advisors when appropriate, has a relationship that no competitor can easily replicate.

Most VRM operators are nowhere near this. But the gap between experience staging and transformation is smaller than it sounds. It starts with designing the annual review as a strategic conversation rather than a performance report. It continues with building the operator's capacity to give genuine property-specific advice. It compounds over time.

---

THE REFERRAL FLYWHEEL

One practical consequence of moving from service delivery to experience staging: owners who had a memorable experience refer other owners.

Service-tier relationships produce satisfied clients. Experience-tier relationships produce advocates. Transformation-tier relationships produce the kind of referral where the owner calls their friend and says "you need to talk to these people, not because they're good at what they do, but because they changed how I think about this property."

The referral flywheel for a VRM company is not built on response times and review scores. It is built on the experiences owners remember and the transformations they felt.
