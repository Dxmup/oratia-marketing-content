COMMODITIZATION IS NOT SOMETHING THAT HAPPENS TO YOU

Pine and Gilmore published The Experience Economy in 1999. The argument they made then is more accurate for VRM operators today than it has ever been.

Commodities are fungible. Goods are tangible. Services are intangible. Experiences are memorable.

Each step up that progression commands higher prices, deeper loyalty, and protection against whoever undercuts your fee. The problem: most VRM companies are stuck in the service tier while calling themselves differentiated.

WHAT SERVICE-TIER DIFFERENTIATION LOOKS LIKE

The management fee you're proud of. The owner portal your software vendor built. The five-star Google reviews.

These matter operationally. But they are not experience differentiators. They are table stakes for being taken seriously in a listing appointment. Every manager in your market has versions of all of them. When every operator presents the same things in the same order, the owner has no rational basis for choosing anyone but the cheapest option.

That is commoditization. And it did not happen to you. You built it.

THE CATEGORY SHIFT

Pine and Gilmore are specific: if you are still charging for the service, you are already competing on price.

For VRM operators, that shift starts in the listing appointment. Not with a better deck or sharper revenue projections, but with the question of what the owner remembers when they walk out.

Most owners remember how the appointment felt. They remember the first question you asked, whether you listened before you pitched, and whether the conversation felt different from the others. They rarely remember the specific fee structure or the software name.

The operator who designs that feeling has moved into the experience tier. The operator who optimizes the deck has not.

WHAT THIS CHANGES

Every touchpoint in the owner relationship is either designed or defaulted. The listing appointment, the onboarding call, the first revenue statement, the first time something goes wrong. Defaulted touchpoints produce forgettable service. Designed touchpoints produce loyal owners who refer their friends.

The next two posts apply this framework to where it matters most: the listing appointment and the first 90 days.

Field note: The operators with the lowest churn in any market are rarely the ones with the lowest fees. They're the ones who've designed enough moments in the owner experience that switching feels like a real loss.
