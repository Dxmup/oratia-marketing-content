THE LISTING APPOINTMENT IS A STAGE

Pine and Gilmore: every business is a stage. The listing appointment is your opening act. Most VRM operators default every element of it.

WHAT OWNERS ACTUALLY REMEMBER

Ask an owner six months after signing why they chose their current manager. The answers are almost never about the fee structure or the software platform. They're about moments.

"She asked me what I was most worried about before she said anything about the company."

"He told me they weren't the right fit for owners who wanted to stay heavily involved. That was the first honest thing anyone said to me in four appointments."

"They left me a one-page summary with three things they'd do with my listing in the first 60 days. Nobody else did that."

These are designed moments. The operators who created them were thinking about what the owner would feel, not just what they would hear.

THE THREE AUDIT QUESTIONS

Pine and Gilmore's framework: eliminate negative cues, reinforce the theme with every element, build in at least one moment the client won't forget.

Applied to the listing appointment:

1. What is your theme? What should the owner feel is true about working with you before they leave the room? If you can't answer this clearly, you don't have a theme. You have a presentation.

2. What negative cues exist in your appointment right now? Vague revenue projections with no methodology. A generic proposal with no property-specific observation. Jumping to fee before you've established value. These undermine trust before the conversation ends.

3. What is the one moment the owner will still remember next week? If there isn't one, the appointment is forgettable.

THE DESIGNED QUESTION

This doesn't require theater. It requires one thing most operators skip: a question the owner wasn't expecting.

Script: "Before I show you anything about how we work, I want to understand something. You've probably been through a few of these conversations this week. What's been missing? What did you leave still wanting to know?"

This signals you listen before you pitch. It surfaces the real concern the owner hasn't been able to articulate. And it makes the rest of the appointment a response to what they told you.

Field note: The Skeptic owner responds most strongly to this pattern. They've been pitched at in every prior appointment. The operator who asks what was missing is the first one to treat them as a person with a specific problem rather than a portfolio unit to be signed.
