This week covered three posts from Chip and Dan Heath's Made to Stick. The common thread: your listing appointment is a communication design problem, not a closing problem. The posts covered the Curse of Knowledge, the concreteness failure, and the story advantage. This newsletter goes deeper on the three principles we didn't have space for in the posts: Simple, Unexpected, and Emotional.

WHY MOST VRM PITCHES FAIL THE SIMPLICITY TEST

Simple does not mean short. It means finding the one thing, stated clearly enough that the owner can hold it.

Here is a diagnostic. After your next listing appointment, call the owner back in 48 hours and ask: "What's the main thing you took away from our conversation?"

If they say your price, your pitch failed.
If they say "you were really nice," your pitch failed.
If they can name the specific thing that makes you different, you are close.

Most VRM operators do not have a clear answer to the question: what is the one thing I want the owner to leave believing? They have a list: responsive, high revenue, good reviews, easy to work with, great photos. A list is not a message. A list is noise.

The operators who win consistently have one thing. One clear claim that everything else in the appointment supports.

Script: "The most important thing I want you to understand about working with us is this: when something goes wrong with your property, which it will, you are never the last to know. Everything else, the revenue, the reviews, the operations, that all matters. But that one thing is the difference between an owner who sleeps and one who doesn't."

That is a core. Not a list.

THE GAP OPENER: CREATING CURIOSITY BEFORE YOU PITCH

The Unexpected principle is the most underused concept in VRM owner acquisition.

Heath and Heath argue that curiosity is created by gaps in knowledge. Before you can close a gap, you have to open one. You cannot be curious about something you did not know was missing.

Most listing appointments start one of two ways: "Let me tell you about us" or "Tell me about your property." Both are reasonable. Neither creates a gap.

A gap opener sounds like this:

"Before I get into what we do, I want to ask you one thing. What's the part of property management that's surprised you most, either positively or negatively?"

This question does several things. It surfaces what the owner actually knows and doesn't know. It tells you which of their assumptions need correcting. It opens the gap between what they expect and what is actually true. And it gives you the roadmap for the rest of the appointment.

If the owner says, "I thought guests would be easier to manage," that is your opening. You now know their gap and you can close it specifically.

If they say, "I expected more communication from my last manager," that is a different appointment. You now know the exact vulnerability in your competitor and where your credibility lives.

The gap opener is not a discovery question for your benefit. It is a gap-opening move for theirs.

THE EMOTIONAL HOOK: OWNER TYPE DETERMINES THE REGISTER

The Emotional principle in Made to Stick is often misread as "make it feel good." That is not what it means. Emotional resonance means connecting to something the audience personally cares about. It is personal and specific, not sentimental.

In a listing appointment, the emotional hook is different for every owner type.

THE INVESTOR owner is not emotionally connected to the property. They are emotionally connected to the financial decision. The emotional hook for the Investor is not the property itself; it is the clarity and predictability of the return. Abstract promises about peace of mind register as soft. Specific projections with credible assumptions register as rational confidence. Do not try to make the Investor feel warmth. Make them feel certainty.

THE STEWARD owner is the opposite. They have an emotional relationship with the property itself. Often it is a family home, an inheritance, or something they built. For the Steward, the emotional hook is care. Not "we treat it like our own," which is a cliche that rings false. The specific behavior that signals care: a photo after every turnover, a direct line to you, a note about something you noticed in the last inspection.

Script: "I noticed when I walked through that you've kept the original fireplace in the living room. We'll document that specifically in our inspection checklist. If anything ever changes with it, that's one of the first things we'll flag."

That sentence costs nothing and builds more Steward trust than 20 minutes of operational explanation.

THE SKEPTIC owner is emotionally connected to their own judgment. They have been burned or almost been burned. The emotional hook for the Skeptic is not reassurance; it is evidence that you understand what went wrong before. Lead with the problem before you lead with the solution.

THE DIAGNOSTIC: RUN YOUR PITCH THROUGH SUCCESS

Before your next listing appointment, run your current pitch through this checklist.

Simple: What is the one thing the owner should leave believing? Can you state it in two sentences?

Unexpected: Does your opening create a gap? Or does it confirm what the owner already expects?

Concrete: Find every abstract promise in your pitch. For each one, can you replace it with a specific scenario, number, or behavior?

Credible: Are you relying on internal credentials (our years in business, our reviews, our awards) or on testable specifics (here is something you can check tonight)?

Emotional: Do you know which owner type you are sitting across from? Is the emotional hook calibrated to what they specifically care about?

Stories: Do you have at least one conflict-and-resolution story in your pitch? Not a testimonial. A story with a problem, a moment, a response, and an outcome.

If you cannot answer yes to each of these, that is where the appointment is leaking.
