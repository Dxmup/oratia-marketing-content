YOUR PITCH IS FULL OF THINGS THAT DON'T LAND

Every VRM operator promises "peace of mind."

No owner has ever signed a management agreement because of that phrase.

Not because they don't want peace of mind. Because the phrase means nothing. It is an abstraction. The owner cannot picture it, evaluate it, or explain it to their spouse. It disappears the moment the appointment ends.

Chip and Dan Heath call this the concreteness problem in Made to Stick. Abstract language is forgettable. Concrete language, grounded in specific details and real scenarios, sticks. This is not a writing principle. It is a cognitive one. Brains process pictures, not concepts.

WHAT ABSTRACT SOUNDS LIKE IN A LISTING APPOINTMENT

"We have excellent communication with our owners."

"Our team is highly responsive."

"We treat every property like our own."

"We provide a hands-off ownership experience."

Every manager in your market says some version of these. The owner processes them as identical. They are, effectively, identical. No mental picture. No differentiation. No reason to choose you.

WHAT CONCRETE SOUNDS LIKE

"You'll get a photo of the property after every single turnover, sent to your phone within 24 hours."

"If something needs attention above $200, you get a call from me personally before we spend anything. Not a text. A call."

"If a guest causes damage, here is exactly what happens in the first two hours: we document, we file, we contact the guest, and we give you a summary by end of day. You're never in the dark."

These are not better promises. They are pictures. The owner can see them. They can describe them to someone else. They can remember them two days later when they are deciding.

Field note: The Steward owner, the one who cares deeply about the property itself, responds most strongly to concrete descriptions of care. Not "we treat it like our own." Tell them specifically who walks through after every clean, what they check, and what happens when something is off. That conversation is qualitatively different from any abstract promise.

THE EXERCISE BEFORE YOUR NEXT APPOINTMENT

Go through your current pitch. Find every abstract promise. Ask one question for each one: what specifically does this look like?

If you can answer that question, put the answer in the pitch and remove the abstraction. If you cannot answer it, that is a product problem, not a communication problem.
