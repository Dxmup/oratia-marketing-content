THE TAX ON EXPERTISE

The more you know about property management, the worse your listing appointment pitch probably is.

That sounds backward. It isn't.

Chip and Dan Heath named it in Made to Stick: the Curse of Knowledge. Once you know something well, you cannot remember what it felt like not to know it. You communicate from your level of understanding, not your audience's. And your audience's starting point is almost nothing.

Most VRM operators walk into a listing appointment with years of channel optimization, cleaning protocol experience, dynamic pricing logic, and operational SOPs running in the background. They know exactly how their operation works and why it works. So they explain it. All of it.

The owner nods along. Politely.

Then they go home and compare your 40-slide deck to the other manager's 10-minute conversation, and they pick whoever felt like a better fit.

THE APPOINTMENT YOU THINK IS GOING WELL

Here is what happens in a typical listing appointment when the Curse of Knowledge is running the show.

You explain your software. The owner doesn't know what Guesty or Hostfully is, but they nod.

You explain your channel strategy. They don't know the difference between OTA mix and direct booking share, but they nod.

You show your occupancy data. They can't evaluate whether 73% is good or bad relative to your market, but they nod.

You cover your cleaning protocol. They think they understand this one. They probably don't, but they nod.

You close with your management fee structure. This part they understand. This is the part they remember.

You have just spent 45 minutes communicating fluently in a language the owner does not speak, and the last thing they heard was your price.

WHAT TO DO INSTEAD

The fix is not simplifying your operation. It is simplifying what you communicate about your operation, in this conversation, with this owner, who has one real question.

That question is not "how does your system work?"

It is: "Can I trust you with my property?"

Those are different conversations.

The operator who strips their pitch to one clear answer to the trust question will outperform the operator who brings the full playbook every time.
